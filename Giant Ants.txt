Giant Ants
Since the Unveiling scattered monstrous life across Calradia, giant ants have proven the most adaptive and invasive of the colossal insects. Workers measure 3–5 feet long, queens larger still, bodies encased in thick, segmented armored exoskeletons that gleam dull black or deep reddish-brown. Mandibles, broad and serrated, crush bone with casual ease—capable of shearing through leather armour or splintering a man’s arm in a single snap.
They excavate vast underground colonies beneath fields, villages, and even town outskirts—networks of tunnels and chambers that collapse barns, swallow livestock whole, and undermine roads without warning. Organized scout parties—lines of ants moving in eerie synchrony—emerge at night to raid surface food stores: granaries, larders, even merchant caravans paused near their territory. When defending the colony they are relentless: waves pour forth, mandibles clacking, formic acid sprayed in stinging jets that blister skin and eyes. Otherwise they ignore humans unless provoked or their nest threatened.
Most common in southern and central warmer zones where soil stays soft and prey abundant; northern colonies are smaller, retreating deep underground to hibernate through winter freezes. They are the most widespread and invasive of their kind—every region has its own strain, from the smaller, swarm-heavy southern variants to the larger, more solitary northern tunnel-diggers.
Villagers tell grim anecdotes of granaries besieged by ants: entire storehouses swarmed overnight, barrels overturned, sacks torn open, grain carried away in endless black streams. Farmers have fended off such raids with brooms, pitchforks, and fire—beating back the tide until dawn, only to find the colony has already tunneled beneath the foundation for the next assault. False-moon nights stir them most: scouts become bolder, acid sprays more frequent, and entire colonies sometimes erupt to the surface in frenzied hunger. In warmer lands, every sudden sinkhole or missing sack of grain carries the same whispered warning: the earth itself has teeth, and it never stops eating.
