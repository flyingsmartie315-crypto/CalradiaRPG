Beastmen
From the Unveiling’s chaotic bleed, some mutant lineages diverged far enough to claim a new identity: the beastmen. Originally spawned from the same warped bloodlines as other mutants—humans twisted by false-moon surges into furred, clawed, bestial forms—they have since forged their own distinct culture and society in Calradia’s ancient forests and deep Rhennod thickets, refined by natural selection amid the wilds. They are no longer mere aberrations; they are a people, savage and proud, calling themselves the “Fur-clad” or “Children of the Wild” in guttural tongues pieced together from old Battanian words and animal cries.
Physically they vary, but all bear the mark of their forest-born mutations: thick fur in mottled browns, blacks, and greys that blends with undergrowth; elongated muzzles with sharp fangs; glowing eyes adapted to low light; clawed hands and feet for climbing and rending. Some walk upright like men, others drop to all fours for speed; heights range from 6 feet to hulking 8-foot alphas. Their bodies are lean and powerful, built for ambush and pursuit—retractable claws, heightened smell and hearing, venomous bites in rare strains. Litters are large and quick to mature; clans breed in hidden dens beneath roots or fallen logs, producing offspring that grow fierce within a year.
Unlike scattered mutant outcasts, beastmen form organized packs with crude hierarchies: alphas lead hunts, shamans read omens in entrails and false-moon shadows, mothers guard the young in fortified lairs. They practice savage religious rituals—crude altars of stacked bones, antlers, and human skulls arranged in spirals mimicking old grove patterns but twisted into mockery. Fresh blood smears bark, feathers and fur woven into fetishes, carved runes (half-remembered Battanian symbols) mark surrounding trees. Captives—human or animal—are torn apart alive while the pack howls in unison, claws raking patterns into earth or flesh. Fians who destroy these sites believe the rites corrupt ancestral worship—beastmen attempting to appease the forest that birthed their curse, or seeking power from dark forces that twisted them.
They are nomadic hunters, following game trails and avoiding open ground. They raid villages on false-moon nights when instincts surge, taking livestock, captives, or supplies. Unlike pure mutants, they have developed a rough code: they do not kill for sport alone, but for survival and ritual. They shun other mutant branches, viewing swamp or cave kin as “lesser” or “broken.” Fians hunt them as sacred foes—arrows seeking the moment a pack exposes itself—but beastmen are cunning, retreating into thickets or ambushing pursuers with traps and howls.
To most Calradians they remain monsters—unsavory, shunned, purged with fire and blade. Yet they are more than deformed humans; they are a people forged by the Unveiling’s dark gift, adaptive and enduring in the wild places. Every whispered rumor of “howls in the deep woods” or “shadows that walk like men” carries the fear that the beastmen have grown bolder, their packs larger, their rituals darker—waiting for the next false moon to howl their claim over the forest they now call home.
