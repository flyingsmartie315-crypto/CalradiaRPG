Mire-Kin
In the fetid southern swamps and river deltas—where water stands stagnant and mist never fully lifts—the mire-kin have evolved as a stable subhuman branch from Unveiling's chaotic mutations, refined by natural selection in the rot. Bodies are elongated and pallid, limbs stretched thin with webbed fingers and toes that propel them through reeds and shallows with sinuous grace. Gills flare along the neck when submerged, extra eyelids protect against stinging insects and murky silt, skin slick and mottled green-gray to blend with algae and mud. Some grow venomous spines along the spine or forearms, dripping toxin that numbs and rots flesh in humid air. Lungs filter foul gases; they can hold breath for hours, emerging only to raid or breed.
These traits breed true through generations: offspring inherit the webbing, gills, and venomous spines, maturing quickly in warm, stagnant pools. Colonies form in half-submerged ruins, floating mats of vegetation, or hollow cypress trunks—breeding grounds where eggs hatch into tadpole-like young that develop in months. Mutations favor endurance in constant rot: heightened smell for carrion drifting on slow currents, saliva laced with mild venom to soften prey, skin that resists leeches and swamp fever. They hunt fish, frogs, waterfowl, and the occasional traveler who strays from the path—dragging victims beneath the surface with webbed hands, drowning them in silence. Riverfolk burn nests when discovered, knowing a single unchecked pair can spawn dozens within a year. False-moon nights drive them to swarm—gills pulsing, eyes glowing in the sickly light—as they hunt closer to boats and villages. To those who live by the water, every ripple or distant splash carries the same fear: the swamp itself has learned to hunger, and its children are many.
