Ghouls
In the shadowed underbelly of Calradia, where crypts and forgotten battlefields fester, something human once crawled down into darkness and never truly came back. Ghouls are the wretched end of that descent—men and women who, driven by starvation or madness, tasted their own kind and found they could not stop. Years in lightless tombs twisted them beyond recognition: bodies hunched and stocky, limbs bent to run on all fours like scavenging dogs, skin stretched tight over corded muscle and yellowed bone. The stench of old rot clings to them, thick and wet, long before they are seen.
Their eyes glow faintly in the dark, pupils blown wide from lifetimes without sun. Jaws unhinge wider than any human mouth should, filled with jagged, stained teeth made for tearing flesh from bone. They hunger only for human meat—fresh or carrion makes little difference—and they will dig through graves or drag down the living to feed. Packs form in the deepest catacombs, breeding swiftly in fetid nests of hair and cloth scraps. A single bite carries plague; a single corpse left unburned can birth more within weeks.
Yet since the Unveiling, ghouls have settled into a grim niche. They are not invaders—they have become part of the ecosystem, a grotesque symbiosis with the living. They clean away the dead from battlefields and plague pits faster than any burial detail, preventing worse rot and disease spread. Villages near old graveyards quietly tolerate them, knowing the ghouls take only the fallen and rarely the living—unless hunger or false-moon madness drives them to break the unspoken balance. To prepared armed men they are still dangerous pests, easily cut down with blade and fire, but they overwhelm the unwary in numbers. The rule remains merciless: cut them down and burn every body they have touched. Leave even one unburned, and the dark will birth another. But the symbiosis endures—ghouls feed on the dead we leave behind, and in their way, they keep the living from drowning in our own decay.
