Move/Disengage: Cautious advance or retreat (1 AP). Allows other actions (e.g., attack after moving). Disengage safely from melee without inviting attacks of opportunity.

Combat Actions
In structured time, entities spend Action Points (AP) on actions declared in Initiative order. Base AP = 3 (modified by factors); each action costs 1–3 AP based on complexity. Half Actions from reference systems map to 1 AP, Full Actions to 2 AP, Varies adjudicated per context (e.g., simple Skill =1 AP, complex =2 AP). Actions can chain if AP allows (e.g., Move + Attack). Use Rule of Cool for dramatic flair on high DoS.
Basic Actions
These are standard options available to all entities unless restricted (e.g., by wounds or gear).

Aim (1 AP): Take extra time to line up a melee or ranged attack, gaining +10 to the next WS/BS test this turn. For ranged, this can represent steadying aim; for melee, studying the foe. Stacks with other bonuses; high DoS on subsequent attack adds extra precision (e.g., ignore 1 AP of armor).
Cast (Varies, 1–3 AP): Unleash a magic spell, Channelling Unveiling energy, or similar ability (requires Channelling skill). Cost depends on spell complexity (simple cantrip =1 AP, major ritual =3 AP or full turn). Roll WP test; DoS determines effect strength, DoF risks backlash (e.g., IP gain or veil-thinning).
Charge Attack (2 AP): Rush up to Charge distance (from table) and make a melee attack with +10 WS and +SB damage on hit. Requires straight path; no difficult terrain. If DoS ≥3, knock target prone or add Fear effect. Fails if interrupted (e.g., opportunity attack).
Disengage (2 AP): Break from melee combat and move up to Move distance away without provoking free attacks. Roll Ag test if outnumbered; failure allows foes 1 free attack at +10 WS. Useful for retreats; high DoS adds evasion (e.g., +10 Dodge next turn).
Move (1 AP): Advance up to Move/Disengage distance (from table), allowing cautious positioning. Can combine with other 1 AP actions (e.g., Move + Attack). In difficult terrain, halve distance or roll Athletics to maintain full speed.
Ready (1 AP): Draw a weapon, pouch item, or prepare an object (e.g., shield up, potion in hand). For ranged weapons, this can include loading if simple (e.g., arrow); complex reloads use Reload. No test needed unless under pressure (e.g., opposed Sleight of Hand).
Reload (Varies, 1–2 AP): Reload a ranged weapon (e.g., crossbow =2 AP, sling =1 AP). Modified by weapon qualities or Talents (e.g., Quick Draw halves cost). Roll BS if hasty; failure jams weapon (1 AP to clear).
Stand/Mount (1 AP): Stand from prone (Ag test if wounded) or mount/dismount a steed (Ride test; failure costs extra AP or falls). On success, move half distance afterward. For beasts like gryphons, add Intimidate test if unruly.
Standard Attack (1 AP): Make one melee or ranged attack using WS/BS (up to Attacks characteristic). Roll d100 ≤ skill; DoS adds damage or effects (e.g., +1 hit location control). Can target specific locations at -20 penalty.
Swift Attack (2 AP): Make two melee attacks (requires Attacks ≥2 or Talent like Ambidextrous). Each at full WS; DoS from first can carry to second (e.g., +DoS damage total). Risks fatigue if overused (WP test or -10 next turn).
Use a Skill (Varies, 1–2 AP): Perform a non-combat skill in combat (e.g., Heal ally =2 AP, Intimidate foe =1 AP). Cost based on complexity; roll relevant skill. Success applies effects (e.g., Charm to rally morale, +10 ally tests). Chained tests possible (e.g., Perception + Search).

Advanced Actions

All Out Attack (2 AP): Commit fully to offense, gaining +20 WS but no Dodge/Parry this turn. On hit, +2 damage per DoS. Risks overextension (if miss, foe gets free attack at +10). Ideal for berserkers or Frenzy state.
Defensive Stance (1 AP): Focus on defense, gaining +10 to Dodge/Parry until next turn but -20 to attacks. DoS on defense tests can counterattack (e.g., riposte on parry). Stacks with armor; useful against multiple foes.
Delay (1 AP): Hold your action until a later point in the turn or the next turn. Specify a trigger (e.g., "when the enemy charges"). When triggered, interrupt and act immediately (roll Initiative tiebreaker if conflicting). Unused Delays carry over but risk surprise (-10 to first test if not triggered). Useful for ambushes or reactions; DoS on related tests adds bonuses (e.g., +10 WS on interrupted attack).
Feint (1 AP): Make a deceptive melee maneuver to unbalance an opponent. Roll opposed WS test; if you win, your next attack this turn ignores target's Parry/Dodge, and gain +10 WS. DoS adds extra effects (e.g., +1 DoS = foe -10 to next test). Fails if opponent has higher Per; DoF allows counter-Feint.
Guarded Attack (2 AP): Perform a cautious melee or ranged attack while prioritizing defense. Make Standard Attack at -10 WS/BS, but gain +10 to Dodge/Parry until next turn. DoS on attack still adds damage; ideal against superior foes. Cannot chain with aggressive actions like Charge.
Jump/Leap (2 AP): Leap over obstacles or gaps up to Jump distance (M × 2 yards horizontal, M/2 yards vertical; test Athletics for extra DoS yards). Costs 2 AP; success allows landing and half Move afterward. Failure risks fall (damage = yards fallen / 2 - TB, min 1). Modified by Size (larger +yards, smaller -yards); Talents like Fleet Footed add +M effective.
Manoeuvre (1 AP): Shift an adjacent opponent 2 yards in any direction (e.g., push into hazard). Roll opposed S test; success moves target without free attacks. DoS adds distance (+1 yard per DoS) or effects (e.g., into fire for damage). Fails if target larger Size; DoF allows reversal.
Parrying Stance (1 AP): Adopt a defensive posture with weapon/shield, gaining +20 to Parry tests until next turn but no attacks. DoS on parries can riposte (free counterattack at -10 WS). Stacks with Defensive Stance; ends if you move more than half distance.
Run (2 AP): Sprint up to Run distance (from table), no other actions except evasion. Gain +20 to Athletics for chases; but -20 to BS against you, and no Dodge/Parry. In terrain, Ag test or fall prone. Ends turn; high DoS adds burst speed (+M next turn if fleeing).

If Players Want to Do Something That is Not Covered by the Actions: Adjudicate per guidelines—use closest action or chain skills (e.g., improvised trap = Set Trap skill, 2 AP). Reward creativity with bonuses (+10–20 for clever RP/setup). If no fit, Varies AP (1–3) and test relevant Characteristic/Skill; DoS scales success (e.g., partial on low DoS).

Combat Movement (Expanded)
There are four different actions that characters can use to move during combat: Move (cautious advance), Charge Attack (aggressive rush), Run (full sprint), and Manoeuvre (positional shift). Jump/Leap handles vertical/horizontal leaps. Hampered terrain halves distances; use tactical map for precision (1 square = 2 yards).
For groups using a tactical map, determine squares equal to Movement in simple terms. Characteristics with a run action triple the Movement for that turn.
Movement in Yards: If unmounted, the character may use animal stats if applicable (e.g., mount adds to M).

Two-Weapon Fighting
Many warriors fight with a weapon in either hand (this includes shields). There are advantages and disadvantages to this style of fighting. The following rules apply when you are fighting with two weapons.
Requirements: Must have Ambidextrous Talent or equivalent (e.g., multi-limbed from Size/Mutation) to avoid penalties. Shields count as weapons for parrying but not attacking unless specialized (e.g., buckler bash).
Advantages: Gain +1 Attack secondary characteristic when dual-wielding (allows extra Standard Attack per turn). Parries gain +10 WS if using off-hand for defense. DoS on main-hand attacks can transfer to off-hand (e.g., +DoS damage total).
Disadvantages: -10 WS/BS to all attacks without Ambidextrous. Encumbrance increases by 50% for wielded items. Cannot use two-handed weapons or heavy shields.
Actions Integration: Swift Attack (2 AP) becomes default for dual-wielders, targeting different foes or same with split DoS. Charge Attack gains +SB damage if both weapons hit.

Optional: Swift Attack Variants
The combat system is designed with ease of play in mind. On a character's turn, he takes his actions, then the next combatant takes his turn and does the same, and so on. This system works well from a playability standpoint, but some players may find they want more realism where multiple attacks are concerned. If the players don't mind the added complication and the GM agrees, one or both of the following optional rules can be implemented:

Staggered Attacks: When a character takes the Swift Attack action, he makes a number of attacks equal to half his Attacks characteristic (rounded down) on his turn. At the end of the round, after all combatants have taken their turns, he can take his remaining attacks. If there are multiple combatants making swift attacks, the end-of-the-round attacks are made in Initiative order.
Declining Accuracy: So many attacks in just one round, a combatant may struggle to maintain his normal level of accuracy. In this variant, the character takes a -10% cumulative WS penalty on each attack after the first. For a minimum WS of 10%! The first attack in a round thus has no penalty, the second -10%, the third -20%, etc.

Grappling
Instead of inflicting damaging, a character can attempt to grapple and immobilize an opponent. A character can attempt a grapple with either a successful Standard Attack or Charge Attack. The grapple is maintained. On the grappling character's turn, he can take one of the following actions. A successful opposed Strength test must be made against the opponent's Strength before any action is resolved. If the test is failed, the grapple is broken.

Inflict Damage: The grappling character can attempt to damage his opponent without a weapon (e.g., choke, headlock). Roll opposed S test; success inflicts SB damage (ignoring armor/TB) + DoS. DoF breaks grapple.
Throw Opponent: Throw the opponent prone up to SB yards away. Opponent takes falling damage if applicable; grapple ends.
Escape Grapple: The grappled opponent can voluntarily end the grapple as a free action. This is a free action, so he can take his normal actions afterward.

A grapple is maintained until the grappling character voluntarily ends the grapple or the grappled opponent escapes. A grappled character cannot take any action that requires movement or use of a weapon. A grappled character can voluntarily end the grapple any time he wishes.
Hit Location Complications
The basic rules for hit locations (see Making an Attack, page 129) are simple enough. They do, however, make two assumptions. First, that characters are fighting a bipedal humanoid, be it Human, Orc, Beastman, or Elf Second, that it is possible to reach all parts of the body when attacking. Obviously, these won't always be the case. When unusual situations crop up, use the following guidelines.

If it is impossible to reach a location with an attack then it is obviously impossible to strike there. A missile attack against a Soldier behind a battlement, for example, could not hit his legs. Similarly, a Halfling couldn't hit a Giant in the head with a sword. In these cases, restrict the hit locations to those that are reachable. If a hit location is unreachable, the GM should apply the hit to the nearest reachable location instead.
If attacking a mounted opponent (a Goblin on a wolf, for example), the player can choose whether to hit the mount or the rider.

Normally, when hitting an attack, roll the Player Damage Total equals 1D10 plus the weapon's Damage rating. When the Player rolls a 10 on the die, the character gets to make a Strength Test. Ranged Attacks
Ranged attacks are made in the same way as melee attacks, with a few exceptions. First, opponents may not dodge or parry a missile attack. Second, characters cannot make ranged attacks if engaged in melee combat, unless armed with a pistol or crossbow pistol. Third, characters must have a line of sight to the target. This means that nothing obstructs the character's view of the target. A character cannot see an opponent in fog, darkness, etc.—he cannot shoot. The Ballistic Skill Test to hit is modified by circumstance. Opponents behind cover, for example, are not partially obscured by mist will be harder to hit. The GM should use Table 6-1: Test Difficulty to adjudicate each ranged attack. Using a missile weapon such as a pistol with the secondary hand imposes a -20% BS penalty. A -20% BS penalty also applies to all shots taken at long range. See Chapter 5: Equipment for details on missile weapons and their ranges.
Shooting into Melee
Sometimes a target will already be engaged in melee combat. This makes the target more difficult to hit. A character can make a ranged attack at an opponent engaged in melee, but suffers a -20% Ballistic Skill penalty.
Extreme Range
Shooting a missile weapon beyond long range is usually a waste of time. It is quite difficult to hit a target so far away with a single shot. In dire straights, however, such shots may be attempted. A character can fire a target up to double long range away, but must take the aim action first. Furthermore, even with the aim, the character suffers a -30% Ballistic Skill penalty to the attack.

Damage and Healing
This section expands Core_RP_Combat.txt with mechanics for resolving damage, critical hits, healing, and long-term effects. Damage reduces Wounds (W); when W ≤ 0, the entity is critically wounded and risks death or permanent injury. Integrate with d100 resolution: successful attacks deal damage = d10 + SB (melee) or weapon base (ranged) + DoS, minus target's TB + Armour Points (AP) on hit location. Use Rule of Cool for vivid descriptions (e.g., high DoS inflicts narrative scars or IP from horror).
Damage
A character can absorb damage up to his Wounds total without penalty. Wounds represent an abstract "buffer level," and only once this has been destroyed is severe damage incurred. That's the good news. The bad news is that taking such damage is rather nasty and can, in some cases, lead to permanent disability and the loss of limbs.
Wounds will go up and down throughout play, as a character takes damage and heals up. Characters require medical attention, which includes the use of the Heal skill or draitghs, when wounded. Depending on how many Wounds a character has left, he is classified as Lightly Wounded or Heavily Wounded. These states are not important during combat, but they do affect the time it takes to heal (see Heal in Chapter 4: Skills & Talents). Characters might also find themselves stunned or helpless.

Lightly Wounded: More than 3 Wounds remaining but fewer than the character's Wounds Characteristic. Without medical attention, the character recovers 1 Wound per day through natural healing.
Heavily Wounded: 3 Wounds or fewer remaining. Without medical attention, the character recovers 1 Wound per week.
Stunned: The character cannot take any actions (even free actions like Dodge). Opponents gain +20 Weapon Skill bonus when attacking.
Helpless: The character cannot defend himself due to incapacitation, or some other circumstance. Attacks against the character automatically hit and inflict an extra 1d10 damage.

Critical Hits
When a character takes more damage than he has Wounds remaining, emanating a Critical Hit is suffered. The attacker rolls percentile dice and cross-references his result with the Critical Value on Table 6-3: Critical Hits. The result will be a number from 1-10, the Critical Effect. Consult the appropriate table for the specified Critical Effect—Arm, Body, Head, or Leg and roll a d10 to determine the outcome. The tables for Critical Effects have been written without any assumptions as to what type of weapon or spell caused the damage. While the tables provide the basics, it's always fun to embellish them with details specific to the attack. No one ever had his head sliced off with a flail for instance, but skills can certainly be split asunder, splattering blood and brains over everyone nearby.
Sudden Death Critical Hits
Sometimes players just want to know if an opponent hits is dead or not and don't much care about the details. Where convenient, the GM can spend combat along without adjudicating detailed critical hits. In other cases, grievous wounds have already been inflicted, so it's just a matter of finishing an enemy off. In these instances, use the Sudden Death option. Roll on Table 6-3: Critical Hits as usual, but ignore the Critical Effects. If the result is 6-10, the subject is slain. Note that due to the nature of the table, any Critical Value of 5 or greater results in death no matter the dice roll.
Example
In the previous example, Jean-Pierre was knocked unconscious with a Critical Hit. Use the Sudden Death rules to see if he survives. The Bretonnian finishes the job. Since Jean-Pierre is helpless, the Bretonnian automatically deals an extra d10 damage. The Ore ends up inflicting another Critical Hit on the Noble with a Critical Value of +4. The GM rolls a 78% on Table 6-3: Critical Hits. +4 means Jean-Pierre has been killed.

Falling Damage
When a character falls, he takes a hit with Damage based on the distance of the drop. See Table 6-10: Falling Damage and round distance up when necessary. Falling Damage is reduced by Toughness Bonus but not armour. If the damage die comes up 10, the character must make another Agility Test or add an extra d10 to the damage. To get up, the character must make a successful Agility Test to keep his grip on anything in his hand as he falls.
A jump is a controlled vertical descent and a jumping character will usually land on his feet. Jumping is a full action. If a character is pushed, or plummets accidentally, he is not jumping but falling.

Jumping and Falling
Jumping is a controlled vertical descent and a jumping character will usually land on his feet. Jumping is a full action. If a character is pushed, or plummets accidentally, he is not jumping but falling. When jumping, the character must make a test if failed, he falls the entire distance. If the character falls the first test, he falls the entire distance. The performer (Acrobat) skill, he may use that instead of Agility at the player's option.
Leaping
A leap is a horizontal jump, like a long jump. It is used to leap from roof to roof or to cross chasms and the like. A leap is a full action.
There are two types of leaps, running and standing.
In a running leap, the character sprints up to his Movement Characteristic x 2 in yards (or Movement in squares). The character must move at least 4 yards (2 squares) in a straight line leading up to take off. The maximum leaped is equal to Movement + Strength Bonus in yards. A successful Strength Test is made. For each degree of failure of the test, the distance leaped is reduced by 1 yard (minimum 1 yard). If the Strength Test is failed by 2 or more degrees, play a minimum leap of 1 yard.
A standing leap is similar, but takes no run-up. This is adjudicated the same way, but the final distance is halved (rounded up).
The vertical distance of a leap (important) is equal to M-2 in yards. Just as with horizontal distance, the vertical distance is reduced by 1 yard for each degree of failure (minimum 1). A result of zero or less means the character gained enough altitude to leap, but not enough to clear any obstacles.
Notes:

Integration: Falling/Leaping costs AP (1 for standing leap, 2 for running/jumping). Modifiers: Size (larger +SB to distance, smaller -SB); terrain (-10 Ag in mud). Unveiling hazards (e.g., veil-drop falls cause mutations on DoF). Chain with Athletics (DoS adds yards). For groups, use slowest M; surprise leaps grant +10 Initiative if successful.
