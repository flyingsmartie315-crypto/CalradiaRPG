Giant Beetles / Stag Beetles
Since the Unveiling scattered monstrous life across Calradia, giant beetles and their stag-horned kin have claimed the warmer southern valleys, fertile plains, and forested hills—rarely venturing north of the Rhennod range where cold snaps kill their larvae and slow their metabolism to a fatal crawl. Horse-sized, they are armoured in thick iridescent carapaces of gleaming black or deep bronze that shift colour in sunlight like oil on water, the plates overlapping in natural plate-mail fashion. Mandibles, broad and curved like scythes or stag antlers, shear through wood beams or light mail hauberks with the ease of shears through cloth; a single bite can sever a man’s arm or split a shield rim to splinters.
Most are herbivorous or omnivorous, feeding on roots, fallen timber, fungi, and the occasional small animal or carrion when opportunity arises. They excavate vast tunnel networks beneath fields, orchards, and village outskirts—cavernous galleries that collapse roads without warning, undermine barn foundations, swallow livestock whole, or open sinkholes beneath unwary travellers. Their burrows are territorial: disturb the soil near an entrance and the beetle surges forth in a thunderous charge, mandibles clacking, legs churning earth like a living siege engine. Otherwise they ignore humans, content to tunnel and feed in peace.
The southern valleys (Neurval, Charas region) and forested hills host the densest populations, where rich soil and mild winters allow rapid breeding. Northern variants are smaller and rarer, confined to sheltered valleys where frost is brief. False-moon nights stir them more than most insects—some claim the sickly light agitates their burrowing frenzy, causing sudden surface eruptions and road collapses that strand caravans or swallow patrols. Farmers curse them as “earth-eaters,” yet quietly tolerate their presence: their tunnels aerate soil and expose buried pests, indirectly benefiting crops in the long term.
To a prepared armed group they are manageable—crossbows and fire-oil drive them back into the dark—but a lone traveler or unprepared village watch can vanish in seconds, dragged screaming into a collapsing tunnel. In the south, every collapsed road or sudden sinkhole carries the same whispered warning: the ground itself has teeth, and it hungers.
