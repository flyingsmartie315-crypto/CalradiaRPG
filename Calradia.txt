Game World: Calradia
Calradia is a vast and diverse continent. To the north lies a peninsula, in the south — a desert, in the west — valleys and coastline, and to the east — steppes and a large lake. Magic is not openly practiced in Calradia, and no organized magical tradition exists among its people. The population is thought to have consisted entirely of humans, however since The Unveiling this is no longer guaranteed. To ordinary men and women, the world is governed by steel, blood, trade, and earthly law. Yet beneath reality lies a deeper layer — subtle, distant, and rarely touched. Most will live and die without ever sensing it.
Calradia is a vast and harsh continent shaped by geography, war, and human resilience. To the north lie fjords, rivers, and a broken peninsula; to the south, a scorched desert of caravans and oases; to the west, fertile valleys and long coastlines; and to the east, open steppes surrounding a great lake.
Calradia is a land where towns smell of blood, smoke, and unwashed sweat, and where the clash of arms is never far away. Roads are churned into mud, strewn with broken carts and the bodies of the unlucky, while merchants struggle onward and children weave between soldiers’ legs, laughing softly despite the danger. Every castle—whether a Vlandian stone keep or a crumbling Imperial fort—is a nest of plots, bribes, and rival claims. Yet even here, life persists: a warm loaf shared at dawn, a flask passed between friends, or quiet words spoken before battle.

Daily Life in Calradia
The medieval era was marked by feudalism, with lords ruling over land and vassals. Most people lived in small villages and worked as laborers, farmers, or craftsmen. Urban life focused on trade and commerce: merchants traveled to sell goods, and artisans produced various items and services.
Life centered on survival and sustenance; food was the main concern for most. Clothing was often handmade and simple, while the wealthy wore more luxurious materials. Education was limited, and only the rich could afford formal schooling.
Entertainment included feasts, festivals, and games such as knightly tournaments and archery contests. Most medicine was primitive — common treatments included bloodletting and herbal remedies. Institutions of scholarly learning or generational learned practices can account for practical, effective treatments within the confines of the practical realities.
Travel was difficult: most people stayed near their homes and only journeyed for necessary reasons, such as trade or war. Many professional warriors — knights or mercenaries — served lords in exchange for pay and participated in battles.

Social Protocol
In Calradia, social standing is strictly observed. Commoners (villagers and townspeople) treat Lords of a town or castle with extreme deference and formal respect.
The role of women in Calradian society is limited; women have few opportunities to achieve power or influence.

The Unveiling
The Unveiling stands as the great fracture in Calradia's history—a singular, universal cataclysm that reshaped the world without a single thunderclap or falling star. Etched into every culture from the Sturgian fjords to the Aserai oases, it bears many names: the "False Dawn" among Battanian druids, the "Moon's Betrayal" in Vlandian-rite sermons, the "Veil's Tear" in Imperial scrolls. Yet all point to that fateful night in 777 BE, over eighteen centuries ago, when the sky itself seemed to hold its breath.
Accounts from across the continent converge like tributaries to a river: a cloudless night sky, not a breath of wind stirring leaf or banner, an icy silence that muffled even the heartbeat in one's chest. Witnesses—from humble herders to Imperial senators—spoke of an inescapable unease, as if unseen eyes peered from beyond the stars, judging and waiting. Then it appeared: a new moon, pale and mocking, cresting the horizon where none should be—a separate celestial body, distinct from the true moon, visible from every corner of Calradia, from the northern peninsulas to the southern dunes. It hung there, unmoving, for hours that felt like eternities, its presence undeniable beside the familiar silver disc already in the sky.
No immediate doom followed—no earthquakes splintering the land, no plagues sweeping the cities. Armies did not fall, empires did not crumble overnight. Yet something had shifted irrevocably. An unfathomable presence had slipped into the world, altering its very fabric in ways subtle and profound.
This intruder moon, dubbed the False Moon in the common tongue shared by travelers and traders across Calradia, now appears sporadically, defying all attempts at prediction or pattern. Its cycle is erratic—sometimes a sliver, other times bloated and looming large, as if pressing close to the earth—always appearing alongside the true moon when both are visible. In times of great disaster or personal turmoil, some claim to glimpse a twisted, grinning face upon its surface, mocking the folly of mortals below. Its glow is no warm light but an eerie absence—a pallid darkness that drains color from the world, more void than illumination.
On nights when the False Moon rises, customs born of terror take hold. Animals are herded into shelters, their restless lowing a warning of the unnatural. Folk huddle behind barred doors, hanging charms of iron, salt, or blessed herbs to ward away evil. Even the thronging streets of bustling cities like Pravend or Galend fall silent from dusk to dawn—no lantern-lit revels, no midnight merchants. The world holds still, as if fearing to draw attention.
For under that sickly light—cast alongside the true moon's silver—Dark Magic gathers in unseen pools—stagnant wells of evil that corrupt flesh, twist the unborn into mutants, and sow seeds of madness and murder. The veil between realms grows gossamer-thin, allowing prophetic dreams to plague the sleeping: visions of lost kin calling from shadows, futures glimpsed in fractured mirrors, or omens of doom that linger like smoke upon waking.
Since that night, Calradia has known rare but persistent disturbances—moments of unnatural silence that swallow birdsong and footsteps; animals refusing well-trodden paths as if sensing invisible barriers; rhythmic knocking from within solid walls, with no hand to cause it; shadows that twist and elongate against the light’s logic; and the quiet, prickling sense of being watched by eyes just beyond sight. These are not constant plagues but fleeting intrusions, reminders that the world is no longer wholly its own.
Yet humanity adapted, as it always does. Life carried on in the mundane rhythms of plow and sword, trade and hearth. The supernatural bleed became part of the tapestry—feared, warded against, but not all-consuming. Kings still waged wars, merchants bartered silks and spices, and farmers sowed fields under the twin moons' watchful eyes. The Unveiling did not end the world; it merely invited the unknown to linger at its edges, waiting for the next false dawn to rise beside the true.
