Drowners
Beneath the still surfaces of Calradia’s ponds, rivers, and swampy backwaters lurks something that was never human. Drowners rise like corpses dredged from the mud—sickly blue-green flesh, bloated and peeling, slime oozing from every pore. Gills flare along their necks, webbed fins slice the water, scales glint dully under torchlight. They reek of stagnant rot and pond-bottom decay, a smell that lingers long after they submerge.
They cluster near villages and river ports, scavenging scraps, dead fish, animal carcasses—anything the current carries. Cowardly by nature, they avoid direct confrontation, content to pick at refuse piles or drag off the occasional drowned animal. But when a lone fisherman wades too deep, or a traveler pauses too long at the water’s edge, the mask drops. They erupt from below in a flash of fins and claws—lightning-fast, silent until the last moment—seizing legs or arms and pulling their prey under. Once submerged, there is no struggle; drowners are patient killers, holding victims down until the lungs give out.
Since the Unveiling, they too have settled into a dark symbiosis. They are not invaders—they have become part of the watery ecosystem around human settlements. They clean the riverbeds and shallows of dead fish, animal remains, and refuse that would otherwise foul the water and breed worse disease. Fishermen and dockhands quietly accept their presence, knowing the drowners take only the careless or the unlucky—rarely venturing far from the water’s edge unless false-moon madness drives them to swarm. Garrisons and riverfolk fight back with nets, barbed spears, and fire-oil poured across the surface, but the creatures always return. They are a persistent, slimy nuisance—cowards that turn deadly the moment isolation offers opportunity. Yet the balance holds: drowners feed on the scraps we discard into the water, and in their way, they keep the rivers from choking on our waste. The wise never fish alone after dusk, and the foolish rarely live to regret it.
