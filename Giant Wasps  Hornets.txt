Giant Wasps / Hornets
Since the Unveiling stirred monstrous life into Calradia’s warmer reaches, giant wasps and hornets have claimed the southern valleys, fertile plains, and forested hills—rarely straying north of the Rhennod range where winter kills their larvae and slows their wings to uselessness. Wing-span stretches 3–4 feet at most for the largest specimens, bodies sleek and segmented in glossy black-and-yellow or deep amber bands, stingers long as daggers and curved like scimitars. Venom is a burning agony—causing rapid, painful swelling that can double a limb’s size and fever that lingers for days, leaving victims shivering and delirious even in summer heat.
They build paper nests the size of small cottages—grey, layered spheres woven from chewed wood pulp—high in treetops, on cliff faces, or beneath rocky overhangs. A single nest houses dozens to hundreds of workers and a single massive queen whose abdomen swells grotesquely in late summer. When undisturbed they forage nectar from flowers and sap from trees, supplementing with small prey: birds, bats, large insects, even unwary squirrels snatched mid-flight. Individually they are weak—easily swatted from the air or crushed underfoot by a determined man—but threaten the nest and the swarm erupts in furious defence. Dozens pour forth in a buzzing cloud, stingers stabbing with mechanical precision, venom flooding wounds in searing pulses. A single sting is painful; twenty or thirty can kill through shock, swelling, and fever before help arrives.
Northern variants are smaller (2–3 ft wing-span) and less active in winter, huddling dormant inside nests until spring thaws revive them. Southern forms—especially in Neurval and Charas valleys—are larger, more aggressive, and active year-round in the mild climate. False-moon nights stir them most: queens lay extra eggs, workers grow restless, and swarms patrol wider, drawn by the sickly light as though it calls to some deep instinct.
To a lone traveler or small patrol they are deadly—the swarm’s numbers turn retreat into rout, venom overwhelms quickly. Prepared groups with fire-oil and smoke can drive them back or burn the nest, but the cost is high: swollen limbs, fevered nights, and the constant drone of survivors circling overhead. Farmers in the south curse them as “sky hornets,” yet quietly leave offerings of sugar-water near known nests to keep swarms placid and focused on nectar rather than livestock or children. In warmer valleys every high rustle or sudden buzz carries the same warning: the sky has teeth, and it stings.
