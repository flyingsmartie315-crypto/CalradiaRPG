Overview
Swadia represents the southern extension of Vlandian territory, characterized by fertile river deltas, coastal bays, and rolling heartlands ideal for agriculture and trade. Bordering the Ebor Peninsula to the north and the Ortysian Basin to the south, it serves as a vital crossroads for commerce along the Trand River and Gulf of Charas. The region blends Vlandian feudal traditions with lingering Imperial influences, featuring knightly strongholds, bustling ports, and shadowed timberlands under the Rhennod Mountains. Early spring thaws bring floods to the lowlands, while false moons stir subtle Unveiling echoes in the mists. Settlements here are fortified against border raids, with economies driven by grain, timber, and river trade.

Settlements
Caleus Castle
Fog-shrouded stone towers rise from cliffside ramparts overlooking hidden coves and marshy lowlands, where smugglers' galleys slip through tidal mists amid grazing cattle and resilient reeds. Markets trade wool and salt fish, knightly oaths echoing across foggy plains with river-fed pools. Vigilant crossbowmen train on battlements facing northern threats, while herders perform lantern rites on stormy nights. False Moon nights summon disorienting echoes in the bay, countered by bonfire wards and blessed charms.

Pravend (Port City)
The ancient Imperial capital, fallen to Vlandian steel centuries ago, yet still wearing the crown of legitimacy like a faded diadem. Massive walls of pale limestone encircle marble forums cracked by time, old aqueducts that still carry water in thin silver threads, and the legendary Pravend Tourney Field—a vast oval of packed earth ringed by tiered stone seating, where the echoes of long-dead emperors once mingled with the roar of the crowd. Lords from every corner of Vlandia flock here for grand jousts of peace and war; the air rings with splintering lances, the thunder of hooves, and the sharp clang of steel on steel. Armories hidden in the lower city produce some of Calradia’s finest articulated plate—heat-treated steel that moves like a second skin, polished to mirror brightness, each suit a fortune in silver groschen and months of labor.
Bound villages beyond the walls supply grain from wide fields and wool from hardy hill flocks, their tithes flowing into Pravend’s coffers to keep the forges hot and the tourney prizes lavish. To the south, along the banks of the broad Trand River, a small but bustling trading port clings to the city’s edge—stone quays and timber slips where flat-bottomed barges and shallow-draft river traders tie up in steady rotation. The port primarily tends vessels navigating the Trand: grain barges from upstream villages, wool-laden boats from the hills, and the occasional merchant galley carrying iron, wine casks, or rare Imperial curiosities salvaged from upstream ruins. Dockhands shout in a dozen accents as ropes are thrown and crates shifted; the air smells of tar, wet timber, and river mud. Small guilds of river pilots and stevedores keep the port alive—practical men who know every bend and sandbar from Pravend to the southern lowlands. The Trand’s sluggish current feeds into a labyrinth of ancient Imperial sewers beneath the lower districts, their dark mouths occasionally exhaling foul air or the faint rasp of drowners that have crept too far upstream. The garrison posts extra torches along the quays and sweeps the accessible sewer grates, but the creatures remain a persistent, slimy nuisance—dragging the occasional careless dockhand or night fisherman beneath the surface.
Yet Pravend’s most extravagant spectacle is rising under the hand of clan Tihr, its current rulers. Baron Aldric and Lady Ethild have poured vast sums of silver groschen—extracted from tolls, markets, and the sweat of bound villages—into a monumental project: the construction of what they intend to be the largest castle-pleasure palace in all Vlandia. Perched on a commanding hill overlooking the Trand and the tourney field, the emerging structure already dwarfs the old Imperial forums: soaring towers of imported white marble and local golden stone, sprawling wings of gilded halls and pleasure gardens, private baths fed by diverted aqueduct channels, and a central keep crowned with domes that catch the sun like captured stars. The palace is designed as both fortress and monument to Tihr excess—defensive walls thick enough to withstand siege, yet inner courtyards open to the sky, filled with exotic plants, fountains, and mosaic floors depicting mythical hunts and feasts. Armories and barracks are tucked discreetly aside; the true heart is indulgence: banquet halls that can seat hundreds, private chambers for favored guests, and hidden vaults for the clan’s growing hoard.
The commons mutter that every block laid is paid for by their hunger; barons watch with envy and disdain, seeing in the palace a brazen claim to greater prestige than even the royal seat at Galend. Derthert has said little—his silence a quiet warning—but the Tihr press on, driven by Aldric’s obsession with legacy and Ethild’s cold calculation that grandeur buys influence where loyalty alone may not suffice. Pravend endures as both trophy and warning: a symbol of Vlandian triumph over the Empire, yet a place where the past refuses to stay buried and the present grows ever more lavish—and precarious. Every lance broken here, every oath sworn, every new tower rising reminds the living that legitimacy is paid for not only in steel, but in the quiet, unending vigil against what still watches from below—and in the glittering ambition that threatens to outshine the crown itself.

Ocs Hall (Town)
One of the few Vlandian towns truly born after the Empire’s fall, cradled in a deep, sheltered valley at the base of the forested Rhennod Mountains. The ragged peaks of Rhennod loom over it like broken teeth against the sky—jagged, storm-scoured ridges that catch the first snow and cast long shadows across the fields below even at midday. Deep forests climb the eastern hills and mountains in a dark, unbroken tide, pines and ancient oaks pressing upward until the slopes grow too steep for anything but rock and mist. The valley itself was once an isolated Battanian holdout, a cratered bowl of rich black soil left by some forgotten volcanic age—its fertile earth still yields heavy harvests of grain and root crops, fed by springs that seep from the surrounding heights.
When the Vlandian warlord Oca—called “Lobster Claw” for the red gauntlet he wore—forced the passes, he claimed the valley in a swift, bloody campaign. The Battanian tribes were driven inland, their songs and rites carried away on the wind, leaving only echoes in the trees. Oca named the smaller lake beside the town Ocspool after himself, a petty mark of ownership that still draws wry smiles from locals.
Stone halls now rise around fertile fields that lap at the crater’s edge—sturdy, square-built structures of dark granite and timber, their roofs steep against mountain snows. Markets thrive in the sheltered squares: grain from the valley bottom, timber felled from the encircling woods, clay dug from rich deposits near the slopes, all traded beneath the watchful eyes of knights who patrol the deep forest fringes. The town lives with one foot in the fields and the other in the trees—its people hardy, practical, slow to trust outsiders. They speak little of the past, but the valley remembers.
The Unveiling left its deepest scar here. Giant bones surface when plows bite too deep or quarries cut new stone—massive femurs taller than a man, shattered skulls with eye sockets like caves—fueling tavern tales of titans slain by ancient druids in forgotten wars. The deep woods that ring the valley harbor very old Wælhreów: ancient pre-conquest mimics with jerky gaits and voices that repeat cries in tongues no living man speaks. They lure the unwary with dead kin’s voices or the bellow of giants long gone; locals bolt doors at dusk, verify every caller with careful questions, and never step into the trees alone after dark. The forests grow treacherous the higher you climb—trails narrow to goat paths, mist thickens until the world shrinks to the next step, and direwolves and bears prowl the upper slopes. Rare but real, these beasts are seldom seen near the valley floor, yet their tracks appear after heavy snow or false-moon nights: massive paw prints, torn saplings, the occasional carcass left half-eaten on a ridge. Patrols speak of eyes glinting in the dark, of low growls that seem to follow without ever closing the distance. Dryad encounters are subtler—whispers in the wind that sound almost like voices, branches that bend without breeze to block a path, or the sudden certainty that the forest itself is watching, judging. Most dismiss them as fancy or fatigue, yet those who linger too long in the high woods sometimes return changed—quiet, distant, eyes always drifting toward the trees as if called.
On false-moon nights the dread thickens—low knocking sounds from within walls, unnatural silence falls over the fields, shadows stretch too long across the cobbles. Direwolves and bears from the high slopes rarely descend to the valley floor, but their presence is felt: a distant howl that carries too far, paw prints circling the outer fields, the occasional livestock kill left torn and uneaten as if for sport. The higher reaches are theirs—wild, untamed, and merciless to any who climb too far without caution.
Yet life persists. Children still chase each other through the streets, women press oil from valley olives, men sharpen blades by firelight. Ocs Hall is a place caught between conquest and memory—where the cratered earth still holds secrets in its rich soil, where the ragged Rhennod peaks watch with silent menace, and where every night the valley reminds its people that some things, once awakened, never truly sleep again.

Verecsand Castle
Rugged stone bastions crown terraced slopes guarding mountain passes, where hardy crops cling to windswept ledges amid craggy massifs and trickling springs. Markets exchange furs and herbs, scar-tales shared in torchlit halls with goat trails winding upward. Knights drill volleys on high grounds, while guides whisper of eyries above. False Moon nights elongate shadows over the massif, countered by avalanche wards and prophetic scars. Sits South East of Ocs Hall, High in the Rhennod Mountains, overlooking the Ocs Apron.
