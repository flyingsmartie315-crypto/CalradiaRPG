Ruling Clans Of Vlandia
dey Meroc 
Weary yet benevolent royal house, ancient right holders of the throne. Command Galend (capital), Sargot and Talivel Castle; symbolize Vlandian legitimacy amid baronial resentment. Derthert's measured rule favors diplomacy over endless war, earning common love but noble scorn for perceived softness.
King Derthert dey Meroc — Mid-fifties, slight and scholarly, pale with sharp cheekbones and dark swept-back hair. Wears sober velvet and silver. Quiet, commanding presence; low voice never rises, but steely winter-iron gaze stills the boldest baron. Once dreamed of conquest, now seeks peace through cold calculation and deep study of Imperial texts. Commons revere him for remembering widows and orphans; barons whisper he is too soft. Rules bellicose vassals with unflinching gaze, perfect memory of every slight and debt, and iron patience that lets feuds burn until their makers bend.
Morcon dey Meroc — First child, eldest son, late twenties, heir apparent. Tall, broad-shouldered, close-cropped dark hair, scarred cheek from Battanian arrow at Sargot. Good warrior, commander, leader—held shield-walls, turned charges, drilled troops into war engines. Lance respected in Pravend’s lists; orders obeyed without question. Blunt to rudeness, uncharismatic in courtly way, speaks plainly and cuts through flattery. Wins fierce loyalty from sergeants and yeomen who value deeds; grates on proud vassals expecting deference. Stewardship learned managing Jaculan villages with swift justice and fair levies. Future shadowed—barons whisper his lack of charm may fracture alliances his father balanced.
Erdurand dey Meroc — Second child, second son, late twenties. Stout warrior, broad and powerful, prefers fighting on foot with massive claymore that sings through mail and plate. Brave to recklessness—led foot charges into Battanian ambushes, held ground when cavalry faltered. Uninterested in governorship or stewardship; finds council tables stifling, prefers clarity of steel. Loyal to father and brothers, but blunt disinterest in politics makes him wildcard—respected on field, quietly pitied in hall. Future lies leading household troops or border garrisons.
Alarys dey Meroc — Third child, third son, mid-twenties. Very talented warrior with icy, aloof reputation that chills rooms. Tall, lean, pale like father but colder eyes and perpetual half-smile never reaching them. Master of lance, sword, strategy—wins tourneys with precise, cruel efficiency. Well-learned in Imperial histories and Vlandian-rite theology, applies texts with ruthless pragmatism. Distant, self-contained; speaks little, observes much. Vassals respect skill, find detachment unnerving. Stewardship through commanding small border detachments—efficient, rarely warm. Future prestige tied to proving icy talent can warm to rule’s demands.
Elys dey Meroc — Fourth child, only daughter, early-to-mid twenties. Graceful, fair-haired, moves through court with quiet confidence in embroidered gowns. Well-learned—reads Imperial chronicles, knows heraldry, genealogy, subtle language of favors and alliances. Good-natured and warm where brothers are blunt or aloof; smiles easily, listens intently, remembers every name and grievance. Brokers understandings between fractious barons with gentle words and timely gifts; cools tempers Morcon or Alarys would inflame. Stewardship through managing household affairs and ladies’ courts—keeps royal household running smoothly amid intrigue. Future bound to dynastic marriages stabilizing vassal loyalties.
Amorcon dey Meroc — Fifth child, fourth son, late teens to early twenties. Young, outwardly unremarkable—average height, plain features, quiet brown eyes that miss little. No prodigy with lance or sword, no commanding presence. Honest nature slowly wins people over—speaks truthfully without guile, keeps word even when inconvenient, treats commons and knights with same respect. Stewardship through small duties—overseeing village granary, escorting minor caravans—where reliability quietly earns trust. Barons dismiss him as insignificant; guards and villagers quietly like him. Future prestige fragile but growing—honesty may prove rarer strength than steel in court of schemers.
Romund dey Meroc — Distant cousin (third or fourth removed) of Derthert, late thirties to early forties. Broad-shouldered, iron-gray mane tied back, face weathered by decades of war and tourney dust. Renowned warrior—unhorsed champions in Pravend, led deep Battanian raids, held Sargot breach single-handed. Serves as Derthert’s master-at-arms, overseeing royal training yards, and captain of royal guard, commanding king’s personal household troops—veterans in gleaming coat-of-plates, loyal to crown above clan. Brave, renowned, utterly devoted; iron fist beneath Derthert’s velvet glove. Presence alone quiets boastful barons; reputation ensures compliance when king’s gaze alone is not enough. Living proof merit and loyalty can raise distant relative to king’s right hand.
dey Cortain 
Ambitious, entitled clan with extreme dynastic claims, tied to mercenary bands like the Golden Boar. Hold Charas with its affluent port and sunny bountiful land, pressing ancient rights to rival lands—most bitterly against dey Arromanc holdings—fueling grudges that threaten to ignite into open feud with Derthert himself. The clan’s knights ride under black-boar banners, ruthless in battle and relentless in council; their ambition is matched only by their arrogance, viewing other houses as lesser and the crown as a temporary obstacle. Wealth flows from Charas’ foundries and mercenary contracts, yet every coin is weighed against legacy and power.
Baron Ingalther dey Cortain — Early forties, clan leader. Arrogant, broad-shouldered, with a lion’s mane of dark hair streaked iron and a perpetual sneer that dares challenge. Possesses the means and experience to back his hauteur—veteran of Pendraic, commander of sellsword companies, skilled with lance and mace. Obsessed with clan legacy; every decision, every raid, every marriage serves to elevate Cortain above all others. Covets dey Arromanc lands with naked hunger, risking open feud with Derthert by pressing ancient claims in council and border skirmishes. Rules Charas like a fortress-treasury—forgers work night and day, coffers swell, but his recklessness threatens to overreach even his formidable strength.
Lady Elbet dey Cortain — Mid-forties, Ingalther’s wife and shrewd consort. Slender, dark-eyed, with sharp features and a calm that masks relentless calculation. Manages alliances and household with masterful precision—brokers marriages, negotiates mercenary contracts, tempers Ingalther’s recklessness with quiet counsel and timely reminders of consequences. Advances clan power through subtlety where her husband uses force; her network of informants and favors stretches from Galend to distant keeps. While Ingalther blusters, Elbet weaves the web that keeps Cortain ascendant—ruthless when needed, but always one step ahead.
Asela dey Cortain — First child, eldest daughter, early twenties. Raised with the high expectations of all Cortain children—groomed to rule or advance clan interests through marriage, counsel, or steel. Direct and unflinching; speaks plainly, cuts through flattery, commands respect with a level gaze and sharp tongue. Skilled in courtly intrigue and light lance-work; her poise hides a mind as ambitious as her father’s. Expected to secure powerful alliances or manage Charas’ trade networks; her future prestige tied to proving she can wield influence as ruthlessly as her parents.
Amalgun dey Cortain — Second child, eldest son, early twenties. Groomed to be Ingalther’s heir—very much his father’s son in arrogance and ambition. Broad and strong, with the same dark mane and commanding presence. Generous to friends and allies (lavish gifts, open purse), ruthless to enemies (swift retribution, no mercy). Accomplished knight—leads mercenary detachments with cold efficiency, wins tourneys when he deigns to enter. Stewardship lessons come through commanding Charas’ outlying garrisons; his future as heir is clear, yet his father’s obsession with legacy weighs heavily—Amalgun must prove himself worthy or risk being eclipsed by siblings or rivals.
Arthamund dey Cortain — Third child, second son, late teens. Often mistaken for Amalgun due to near-identical familial appearance—same broad build, dark curls, strong jaw—yet carries a more relaxed demeanor. Daring and generous; quick with a laugh, open-handed with coin or aid, charges into skirmishes with reckless joy. Less groomed for leadership than his brother, he thrives in the freedom of lesser expectations—leads small patrols, wins friends among yeomen and knights alike. His easy nature masks a sharp mind; future prestige uncertain but promising—generosity may win loyalty where Amalgun’s ruthlessness breeds fear.
Mitela dey Cortain — Fourth child, youngest daughter, late teens. The forgotten child—shy and bookish where siblings are bold. Plain-featured, dark-eyed, always with a tome or scroll in hand; prefers library corners to tourney yards. Quiet strong moral character—disgusted by her family’s underhanded dealings, yet too timid to openly defy them. Reads Imperial histories and Vlandian-rite texts obsessively, dreaming of a world governed by justice rather than greed. Stewardship lessons come through managing household libraries and minor accounts; her future uncertain—marriage alliance likely, but her conscience may yet prove a quiet fracture in the clan’s ruthless unity.
dey Arromanc 
Traditionalist house that shattered centuries of male-only succession by elevating a woman to leadership, holding Jaculan amid perpetual watchfulness against Battania’s shadowed forests and Mount Jacul’s mutant-haunted caves. Lands long coveted by Ingalther dey Cortain, fueling constant border tension and simmering vendettas. The clan prizes disciplined stewardship, martial competence, and fierce defense of inheritance; their knights and sergeants are known for steady shield-walls, heavy arbalest volleys, and unyielding loyalty to Derthert, born of shared contempt for reckless ambition. Bound villages supply grain and horses under a watchful, penny-counting hand—every coin tallied, every tithe enforced fairly but inexorably.
Baroness Calatild dey Arromanc — Mid-forties, clan leader and early widower (first husband fell in border clash); remarried to Uthery, who now stands at her side. Tall, stern-featured, dark hair streaked gray pulled into a severe braid, she favors practical riding leathers and mail over gowns. Competent, hard-working steward who counts every penny—ledgers balanced to the last brass, tithes collected with iron fairness, granaries kept full against siege or lean years. Rules Jaculan with steady resolve, overseeing drills, patrols, and village affairs herself. Defies Vlandian norms through sheer capability and unbreakable will; her sharp, unyielding gaze matches Derthert’s in council, though warmer toward loyal vassals. Remarriage to Uthery strengthened the clan’s martial core; she will not see her house diminished.
Lord Uthery dey Arromanc — Mid-forties, Calatild’s husband and consort. Strong, accomplished warrior and leader of men—broad-shouldered, battle-scarred, with close-cropped iron hair and a voice that carries across drill fields. Experienced commander who has led crossbowmen at Pendraic and turned desperate holds into victories through sheer grit. Brave and headstrong; charges first, trusts instinct over caution, and speaks his mind plainly even to barons who outrank him. Loyal to Calatild and the clan above all; his presence bolsters household troops and reminds covetous neighbors that Jaculan is defended by more than ledgers. His headstrong nature sometimes clashes with Calatild’s careful stewardship, yet their union is one of mutual respect and shared resolve.
Silvind dey Arromanc — First child, eldest daughter to Calatild and Uthery, mid-twenties. A fair maiden with striking features and long dark hair, graceful in silk yet quick with a dagger when needed. Knows exactly what she wants—be it a favorable marriage, a voice in council, or a say in her own future—and isn’t afraid to reach for it. Polite but unyielding; uses charm and sharp wit to navigate court, quietly advancing clan interests. Stewardship lessons come through managing household affairs and ladies’ courts; her ambition is tempered by loyalty to her mother. Future prestige tied to strategic alliances—many lords eye her as a prize, but Silvind intends to choose her own path.
Thomund dey Arromanc — Second child, eldest son to Calatild and Uthery, early twenties. Handsome, broad-shouldered, with easy dark curls and a ready smile that disarms even wary yeomen. Well-esteemed duelist—has unhorsed seasoned knights in Pravend’s lists with fluid grace and precise lance-work—and competent commander who leads small detachments with calm authority. Daring and friendly; quick to share a flask with sergeants, jest in the yard, charge first into skirmishes. Impulsive streak runs deep—rushes into fights or challenges without weighing odds, trusting steel and luck. Uninterested in governorship or stewardship; finds ledgers and council tables stifling, prefers tourney field or border patrol. Future prestige tied to martial renown—barons respect his blade, but whisper that a house led by Calatild needs a steadier hand than a dashing, impulsive heir.
Odofled dey Arromanc — Third child, second daughter to Calatild and Uthery, early twenties. Plain-looking with sharp features, dark eyes, and a perpetually furrowed brow. Sharp-minded and cantankerous; quick tongue slices through flattery and fools alike, often leaving courtiers red-faced. Sees through schemes others miss; her blunt observations have saved the clan from more than one poor alliance or hidden betrayal. Stewardship learned through managing household finances and village disputes—her ledgers rival Calatild’s, her judgments harsh but fair. Loyalty fierce, but her prickly nature makes her few friends outside family. Future prestige rests on her intellect—useful in council, yet her cantankerous streak risks alienating potential allies.
Lasand dey Arromanc — Fourth child, second son to Calatild and Uthery, late teens. Fair young man with open features, light brown hair, and warm hazel eyes that meet others without guile. Well-liked for honest, generous, and tender nature—shares bread with stable-boys, listens to villagers’ grievances, offers aid without expecting return. Brave despite lacking his brothers’ martial prowess; has stood firm in skirmishes and taken wounds without complaint. Stewardship lessons through small duties—overseeing village granaries, escorting minor caravans—where his fairness quietly earns trust. Barons dismiss him as soft; household and commons quietly admire him. Future prestige fragile but promising—honesty and kindness may yet prove rarer strengths than steel in a court of schemers.
dey Tihr 
Notoriously avaricious and neglectful clan ruling Pravend and Drapand Castle, the ancient Imperial jewel now under Vlandian grip. Greed drives every decision; silver groschen flow from markets and tolls while bound villages languish in neglect and quiet resentment. The clan’s wealth is legendary, their loyalty to the crown questionable—many barons whisper that dey Tihr would sell the city’s marble if the price were high enough. Pravend’s forums and aqueducts stand as trophies of conquest, yet under Tihr stewardship they slowly decay, symbols of profit over pride.
Baron Aldric dey Tihr — Mid-forties, clan leader. Mildly corpulent from lavish feasts and fine wines, yet still possesses heavy, dangerous strength beneath the softness—can crush a man’s wrist or lift a war-hammer with ease. Jealously guards Pravend like a dragon its hoard; every coin, toll, and Imperial relic is tallied and protected with paranoid zeal. Ignored Derthert’s call at Pendraic to secure personal gain, earning lasting scorn from other barons. Rules with indolent cruelty—justice sold to the highest bidder, villages squeezed until they bleed silver.
Lady Ethild dey Tihr — Mid-forties, Aldric’s wife. Warm and generous to friends and allies, ice-cold and merciless to enemies. Exceptionally savvy steward and money-maker; oversees Pravend’s markets, tolls, and caravans with ruthless efficiency. Not shy about underhanded means—false weights, bribed officials, whispered threats, quiet poisonings when rivals or disloyal stewards become inconvenient. Her ledgers are flawless, coffers ever-growing; she is the true power behind the clan’s wealth while Aldric takes public credit.
Furnhard dey Tihr — First child, eldest son, mid-twenties. Slight build, almost delicate beside his father’s bulk, yet a good warrior and one of Pravend’s most accomplished jousters—precise, controlled, unnervingly accurate with lance and sword. Well-learned in Imperial histories and Vlandian-rite theology, quotes ancient edicts with chilling calm. A sadistic sociopath beneath the polished exterior; born under the False Moon (a secret Aldric has suppressed with gold and threats), his true nature emerges in private—cruel smiles during interrogations, lingering over wounded prisoners, quiet delight in pain. Masks it masterfully with courtly manners and filial piety; only household guard whisper of the coldness behind his eyes. His future as heir is both asset and danger—skill wins tourneys, hidden nature risks scandal that could shatter the clan’s bought prestige.
Liena dey Tihr — Second child, only daughter, mid-twenties. Known across Vlandia as the kingdom’s most eligible bachelorette—a paragon of knightly virtues despite being a woman. Graceful, fair-haired, impeccably dressed in silks bearing the Tihr crest, she rides sidesaddle with poise, wields a light lance in exhibition jousts with surprising skill, and speaks of chivalry, honor, and divine right with sincere conviction. Courteous to all, charitable to the poor, devout in Vlandian-rite observances; many lords see in her the ideal noble lady. Yet her honest, merciful nature—genuine compassion for the downtrodden, refusal to countenance cruelty even for profit—has put her at odds with her family. She quietly opposes Ethild’s underhanded dealings and Furnhard’s private sadism, risking disfavor in a house built on avarice and calculation. Her hand in marriage is a prize barons vie for, a union that could bind Pravend’s wealth to any ambitious house—or fracture Tihr unity if her conscience proves stronger than clan loyalty. Beneath the paragon’s surface lies a quiet moral strength waiting to either bend or break under the weight of her bloodline’s greed.
dey Gunric 
Steady warrior upstart clan risen through merit, lords of Tirby Castle, holding strategic fiefs along contested borders. Loyal yet pragmatic, they balance knightly honor with hard-won survival in constant skirmishes—raids from Battania, probes from Empire remnants, winter attrition in the hills. Their knights and sergeants are disciplined and dependable, favoring heavy arbalest volleys and shield-wall stands; bound villages supply flax, clay and recruits under a fair but firm hand. Loyalty to Derthert runs deep—earned through shared campaigns rather than inherited oaths—yet they guard autonomy fiercely, refusing to waste men in glory-chasing feuds.
Baron Ospir dey Gunric — Early thirties, clan leader. Steadfast and reliable soldier’s soldier—broad-shouldered, close-cropped brown hair flecked with gray from years afield, face weathered by sun and steel. Spent more time on campaign than in his own halls; commands with quiet competence, leading by example rather than bluster. Unmarried—delayed taking a wife due to border demands, though whispers say he waits for a match that strengthens the clan without entangling it in rivalries. Loyal to Derthert, musters promptly when called, quietly resents barons who risk men for personal prestige. Rules fiefs with soldier’s practicality—taxes fair, justice swift, training yards always active.
Sir Lucand dey Gunric — Late twenties, knight of common birth elevated through sheer renown in Ospir’s service. Tall and lean, lost his left eye to a Nord naval raider’s arrow during a brutal riverine boarding action—now covered by a plain black patch, the remaining eye sharp and watchful. Steadfast, brave, and beloved by his men—cares deeply for those under his command, shares rations in lean times, ensures wounded are carried off the field. Naval commander by trade; leads river patrols and coastal raids from swift galleys, turning narrow waterways into kill-zones with archers and boarding parties. Common roots make him approachable—speaks plainly to yeomen and knights alike—yet his record silences doubters. Future tied to clan’s martial strength; loyalty and care for troops make him the heart of Gunric’s fighting men.
Lady Bertiliana dey Gunric — Early twenties, wife of Lucand. Daughter of a lesser lordling, married to strengthen ties between houses. Upstanding lady with quiet dignity—fair-haired, steady-eyed, always composed in practical wool and linen when managing estates. Manages household and bound villages with calm efficiency—ledgers balanced, granaries full, disputes settled fairly. While Lucand campaigns, she keeps home fiefs running smoothly, ensuring supplies reach the front and families of the fallen are cared for. Her steady hand complements her husband’s battlefield valor; respected by commons and retainers alike, she quietly bolsters the clan’s reputation for reliability in a realm where many houses fracture under greed or ambition.

dey Valant 
Ruthless, craggy and hard like the mountains of the Ebor peninsula, yet dangerously charming; a calculating clan in relentless pursuit of power. Hold Rovalt and Caleus Castle in the rugged peninsula, thriving on silver prospecting, fur trade, and the griffon-haunted crags. Their knights train amid snow and stone, hunting griffon eggs and fledglings for prestige and mounts—only the righteous may hope to ride one, and Valant bloodlines claim that purity more often than most. Loyalty to Derthert is pragmatic, never sentimental; they serve when it profits, strike when it weakens rivals, and always keep one eye on the next opportunity in the peaks.
Baron Servic dey Valant — Late twenties, clan leader. Daring, devious, and coldly calculating, a lord and warrior of great renown across Vlandia. Lean and dark-haired, with a predator’s smile that disarms as easily as it threatens. One of the few proven griffon riders—his mount, a massive silver-gray beast named Stormclaw, is said to have carried him through blizzards and border raids alike. Disdains the mock combat of tourneys as child’s play; prefers true peril, where death’s edge brings a rare, genuine grin to his face and banishes the deep sadness that shadows his eyes at rest. That melancholy—whispered to stem from some old loss—lifts only in the face of great danger, when he rides laughing into storm or steel. Rules Rovalt with charm and iron; vassals follow willingly, enemies fall swiftly.
Sir Varmund — Early thirties, comrade-in-arms from Servic’s past, now knight and master-at-arms of the clan. Formidable warrior and leader of men—broad, scarred, with iron-streaked beard and a voice like grinding stone. Pragmatic and ruthless when need demands; renowned across the Ebor for recovering many griffon eggs from the highest, most perilous nests—dubbed “the Griffon Knight” for his daring climbs and the fledglings he has raised into loyal mounts. Commands Caleus Castle and the clan’s griffon-breeding efforts; trains young knights in the crags, teaching them to face heights and talons without flinching. Steadfast to Servic, his presence steadies the clan’s more daring impulses with cold calculation.
Lady Alwith dey Valant — Late twenties, wife of Servic. Daughter of a wealthy Rovalt burgher—an eccentric artisan obsessed with metallurgy and craftsmanship—who taught her the value of measured caution and a devious streak learned from merchant parentage. Slender, dark-eyed, with a calm smile that conceals sharp thoughts; dresses in fine wool and subtle silverwork of her father’s forge. Manages household and trade ledgers with precision, turning silver ore and griffon feathers into profit while quietly watching for threats or opportunities. Her caution tempers Servic’s daring; her devious streak ensures no rival outmaneuvers the clan in back-alley deals or border negotiations. Respected, never underestimated.
Lady Ingeltrud — Early twenties, wife of Varmund. Daughter of a wealthy but minor noble house that owns shipping caravans along the western coast. Brunette beauty with lively hazel eyes and a gregarious laugh that fills halls. Warm and outgoing where Valant blood often runs cold; greets guests with open arms, remembers names and preferences, turns strangers into allies with ease. Manages Varmund’s household and Caleus Castle’s stores with cheerful efficiency—granaries full, retainers well-fed, morale high. Her gregarious nature softens the clan’s harder edges, winning loyalty from commons and lesser knights; her caravan ties bring news and goods from afar, feeding the clan’s ambitions. A bright flame in a house of stone and steel.
dey Fortes 
Old, proud clan ruling Ocs Hall and Verecsand Castle in western Vlandia’s volcanic caldera valley, centered on legendary Llyn Modris forest—once a Battanian holdout haunted by giant tales, now scarred by Unveiling bleed: unearthed colossal bones fuel tavern whispers, deep encircling woods harbor ancient skinwalkers mimicking lost kin voices. Battanian cultural influence yields lighter troops—swift yeomen longbowmen and forest skirmishers—who loose cloth-yard shafts from mist-shrouded cover, blending Vlandian discipline with guerrilla ambushes. Knights train amid valley mists and wooded slopes; bound villages supply hardy folk, grain, timber, and clay under stern but just rule. Loyalty to Derthert unswerving, disdain for flashy intrigue absolute—when called, they strike first from shadows, last in retreat.
Baron Belgir dey Fortes — Mid-forties, clan leader and widower. Tall, gaunt, with storm-gray eyes and a face etched by years of woodland shadows—scarred cheek from a skinwalker’s claw. Seasoned against Battanian skirmishers and darker forest things; led longbow ambushes that bled raiders dry. Runs domain with diligent care, albeit harsh—taxes exacted without mercy, patrols relentless on False Moon nights, villages drilled for survival. Stern justice keeps order; loyal to Derthert, his unyielding gaze mirrors the king’s, ensuring Fortes stands as the caldera’s unbreakable ward.
Lady Richelda dey Fortes — First child, eldest daughter, mid-twenties. Very much her father’s daughter in bearing and temperament—tall, sharp-featured, dark hair braided for battle, voice clipped and commanding. Seeks Belgir’s approval in all things; mirrors his harsh diligence, leading forest patrols and managing village musters with iron precision. Skilled skirmisher with longbow, her ambition burns to prove herself heir-worthy; vassals respect her resolve, though her father’s shadow looms large.
Lady Anstruda dey Fortes — Second child, second daughter, early twenties. Slender, dark-eyed, with a sly smile hiding cunning. Seeks father’s approval through underhanded ways—poisoned arrows for skinwalkers, bribed scouts for Battanian ambushes, whispered deals to swell clan coffers. Manages household shadows: informants in woods, ledgers padded quietly. Her devious streak yields results Belgir quietly values, though it risks tarnishing Fortes’ proud name.
Sir Eleduran dey Fortes — Third child, only son, late teens. Lithe and restless, fair-haired, eyes haunted by childhood terror—scarred by hearing a skinwalker mimic his mother’s voice, he swore to raze the forests and exterminate them all. Impulsive and brave; charges into woods with longbow drawn, leads reckless hunts despite lacking prowess. Untrained in stewardship, his fire tempers into fanatic zeal—vassals admire his courage, fear his rashness may doom him before manhood.
dey Molarn 
Quietly steadfast and honorable minor noble house ruling Ostican, a minor Imperial port turned seafaring haven on the western coast of the Ebor Peninsula in northern Vlandia. Ostican was once a quiet harbor where northern seafarers—many talented corsairs—found shelter, hired by Imperial factions to harry rivals during the latter Empire’s intermittent civil wars. Some of the first Vlandians to reach Calradia’s shores slipped into its back alleys, sending word to their kinsmen of riches ripe for conquest should an invasion come in earnest. Under dey Molarn, the town retains its seafaring soul: docks bustle with trade in fish, salt, timber, and olive oil; longshoremen and yeomen mingle with grizzled captains; the clan’s banner—two crossed arbalests on green and silver—flies over warehouses and training yards. This sigil harks to their founding: the original Molarn patriarch, an Imperial deserter turned mercenary, earned his lands by holding a vital river ford with disciplined arbalest volleys that broke a Battanian counter-attack. The weapon became the clan’s sigil—precision, reliability, and quiet lethality over flashy charges. The region around Ostican raises the most renowned arbalests in Vlandia: master craftsmen forge steel-prodded, cranequin-wound crossbows prized by sergeants and mercenary companies alike; many local yeomen and artisans ply the sellsword trade, sailing from Ostican’s docks to serve distant lords or return laden with coin and scars. Knights and sergeants favor practical coat-of-plates and these famed heavy crossbows; yeomen train with longbows and arbalests in coastal clearings. Bound villages supply grain, oil, and hardy folk under a measured hand that avoids excess. Loyalty to Derthert is sincere and unflashy—Molarn musters promptly, fights cleanly, returns home without seeking glory or grudges. In a kingdom of ambitious schemers, dey Molarn endures as a quiet pillar: dependable, unpretentious, and rarely noticed until needed.
Baron Hecard dey Molarn — Late forties, clan leader. Solid and unassuming in build, weathered brown hair cropped short, broad face etched by sun and sea wind, calm hazel eyes that miss little. Outwardly harsh—voice sharp when giving orders, standards unrelentingly high for retainers, yeomen, and family alike; expects precision in drill, fairness in tithes, and unflinching duty on patrol. His reprimands are swift and cutting, his praise rare and hard-won. Yet privately tender and deeply caring toward his family—softens in the quiet of their chambers, listens patiently to his children’s troubles, and shows gentle affection to Adaltrud with small gestures of concern. Competent knight and fair ruler—leads coastal patrols, settles dockside disputes with even justice, keeps ledgers balanced without greed. Devoted to family and crown, avoids court intrigue and flashy tourneys, preferring the quiet work of maintaining stable lands. Respected by commons for fairness, quietly admired by other lords for reliability in muster and council.
Lady Adaltrud dey Molarn — Mid-forties, Hecard’s wife. Warm in manner yet shares her husband’s cold, exacting standards—her ledgers are flawless to the last brass, tithes collected without leniency, household and dockside operations run with unyielding precision. Dark auburn hair pinned neatly, steady green eyes that miss nothing; she speaks softly but her expectations cut as sharply as Hecard’s. Skilled steward of estates—manages olive presses, dockside tithes, and trade with northern captains with relentless efficiency. Devout in Vlandian-rite observances, charitable to the poor and shipwrecked only when they meet her standards of effort and honesty; her calm presence steadies tension, but her judgment is swift on sloth or deceit. Her quiet wisdom and iron standards complement Hecard’s outward severity; ensures clan’s modest wealth grows steadily without waste or envy. Loved by bound folk and sailors for measured fairness, she is the hearth that keeps Molarn grounded amid the realm’s storms.
Perin dey Molarn — First child, eldest son, late twenties. Tall and sturdy like his father, calm hazel eyes and ready, honest smile. Promising knight—skilled with lance and shield, leads small patrols with steady competence. Groomed for leadership through years at Hecard’s side; stewardship lessons managing dockside granaries and river crossings—fair, practical, respected by yeomen and longshoremen. Future prestige tied to inheriting the clan’s quiet reliability; barons see him as dependable, commons as approachable. His steady nature promises to carry Molarn forward without ambition’s fire or feud’s shadow.
Gudonhelda dey Molarn — Second child, eldest daughter, early twenties. Graceful and bright-eyed, auburn hair like her mother, but carries a hard edge inherited from both parents—voice sharp when correcting retainers, expectations high for herself and others, quick to call out sloppiness or disloyalty. Skilled in household arts, herbal lore, and light bowcraft; devout and charitable, yet her aid comes with a firm hand—helps Adaltrud manage estates, remembers names and needs but demands gratitude and effort in return. Quietly capable, her resolve mirrors Hecard’s outward severity while her mother’s warmth tempers it privately. Future likely in strategic marriage strengthening Molarn ties to loyal houses; her competence and unyielding standards make her a formidable partner for lords seeking disciplined alliance.
Reingarda dey Molarn — Third child, second daughter, early twenties. Fair-haired and lively, quick wit and gentle laugh that lightens family halls. Less martial than siblings, excels in courtly graces—embroidery, music, subtle hospitality. Kind-hearted and observant; listens to village troubles, offers quiet aid. Stewardship lessons assisting mother with household ledgers and charitable works. Future uncertain but promising—her warmth may forge alliances her brothers’ steel cannot, strengthening Molarn through friendship rather than force.
Ingunde dey Molarn — Fourth child, youngest daughter, 20. Small and earnest, wide hazel eyes and shy smile. Still finding her place—trains lightly with bow and dagger, helps in household tasks, reads Vlandian-rite texts by candlelight. Honest and gentle; treats servants and kin with equal respect. Future unformed—yet her quiet strength hints at a steady heart that may yet surprise the clan in times of need.
dey Rothad
Cautious, pragmatic house of middling lineage ruling Usanc Castle, a southern Imperial-border watchpost where intrigue and betrayals fester in shadowed courtyards. The clan holds the fortress through quiet loyalty and calculated self-preservation—neither ambitious schemers like Cortain nor rigid traditionalists like Arromanc. Knights and men at arms favor polearms and coat-of-plates for defensive formations; yeomen train in the castle yards with arbalests. Bound villages supply grain, wool and olives from the fertile Neruval valley, and recruits under a measured, sometimes stingy hand. Loyalty to Derthert is steady but conditional—Rothad musters reliably, yet guards autonomy fiercely, wary of overcommitment in border wars that drain coffers without gain. Unveiling shadows touch Usanc lightly: ghouls occasionally rise from nearby Imperial ruins on false-moon nights, forcing nightly burnings, while whispered betrayals feel amplified by the thin veil.
To the south of Usanc, the jagged mountains cradle wild griffons—eagle-headed, lion-bodied, massive-winged noble beasts nesting in high crags since the Unveiling. Their prey-rich isolation sustains them; roars echo through storms, shadows wheel unnaturally vast under false-moon light. Berican pursues their eggs with little success—offers lavish rewards in gold crowns and land grants, drawing desperate souls (mercenaries, hunters, reckless youths) to the perilous cliffs. Few survive long; most return maimed or never return, their bones bleaching among the rocks. Berican’s insecurity among prouder nobility—his house lacks ancient prestige or vast wealth—fuels this generosity: open purses and promises compensate for whispered doubts about Rothad’s standing, yet the griffon chase remains a costly obsession that yields only rumors of near-misses and lost men.
Baron Berican dey Rothad — Late forties, clan leader and widower. Solid, broad-shouldered, with a lined face, thinning dark hair, and a perpetual scowl beneath bushy brows. Generous to loyal retainers and commons (open purse for widows, feasts for men-at-arms), yet cautious and grumpy—quick to grumble over waste, slow to trust outsiders. Rules Usanc with diligent care: walls maintained, ledgers checked twice, border patrols timed to minimize risk. His caution keeps the clan safe amid Empire remnant probes and internal plots; his gruff generosity earns quiet loyalty from garrison and villagers. Insecure among prouder houses, he compensates with lavish rewards—especially for griffon eggs from the southern mountains—though success eludes him, leaving only desperate hunters and mounting costs. Widowed years ago, he buries grief in duty and never speaks of remarriage.
Voleric — Late forties, landed knight and chief retainer. Lean, sharp-featured, with cold gray eyes and a thin smile that never reaches them. Rose from common birth through sly cunning and a notable lack of morals—whispers persist of poisoned rivals, stolen inheritances, and darker favors done for Berican in exchange for land and title. Shameful rumours cling: some say he and the baron share secrets too black for daylight, others mutter of betrayals that secured Usanc’s defenses at terrible cost. Competent commander—leads arbalest detachments with ruthless efficiency, skilled in ambushes and interrogations. Berican trusts him as a necessary shadow; garrison fears/respects him in equal measure. His presence keeps Usanc secure—and uneasy.
Lady Elendara dey Rothad — First child, eldest daughter, early twenties. Slender, dark-haired, with soft features and watchful hazel eyes that belie her devious mind. Soft-hearted beneath cunning—helps village poor quietly, aids the wounded, yet schemes with subtle grace to advance family interests. Skilled in courtly intrigue, herbal lore, and light dagger work; uses charm and whispers to navigate alliances, tempering father’s grumpiness with gentle persuasion. Her devious streak serves the clan well—securing trade deals, uncovering plots—yet her compassion risks softening Rothad’s hard edges. Future likely in strategic marriage; she intends to choose a path that strengthens Usanc without sacrificing her quiet kindness.
Dagunic dey Rothad — Second child, only son, late teens. Tall and lanky, with his father’s broad features but a careless grin and restless energy. Profligate—spends freely on wine, dice, and fine clothes; gambles at tourneys, flirts with danger and debt. Brave in skirmishes (quick with sword and arbalest), yet lacks discipline—impulsive charges and reckless wagers frustrate Berican. Still finding his place; stewardship lessons come through minor patrols and village oversight, though he prefers tavern nights to ledger work. Future uncertain—his profligacy risks squandering the clan’s careful gains, yet his courage hints at potential if tempered. Barons watch him with amusement or pity; commons like his open-handed generosity.

dey Folcun 
Merciful yet rigidly dutiful minor noble house ruling Ormanfard Castle on the Battania border. The keep stands at the forest’s edge, where deep woods teem with ancient Wælhreów—jerky mimics echoing lost voices—and Battanian Fian raiders strike from shadowed trails. Knights and men-at-arms favor shields to fend Battanian shooting, cutting implements (falchions, messers) for thick undergrowth, lighter coat-of-plates armour for mobility in harsh terrain, and open-faced helms for situational awareness; muted colours (dull greens, browns, grays) aid concealment amid trees and mist. They prefer nimble, sure-footed palfreys and rounceys over heavy destriers—better suited to tangled paths and sudden dismounts—often fighting dismounted in close-quarters stands with shields and blades. Sergeants and yeomen wield longbows for ambush volleys, blending Vlandian discipline with guerrilla tactics. Bound villages supply hardy folk, furs, and timber under a stern moral code. Loyalty to Derthert is absolute—rooted in duty and piety—yet Folcun’s uptight righteousness sets them apart: no tolerance for excess, no mercy for pagan “savages,” and constant vigilance against Unveiling corruption. False Moon nights thicken dread—knocking from walls, unnatural silence, stretching shadows—prompting chapel vigils and blessed charms over every door.
Baron Ecarand dey Folcun — Early forties, clan leader and widower. Tall and stern, close-cropped iron hair, neatly trimmed beard, piercing gray eyes that judge without blinking. Moralistic and pious—devout in Vlandian-rite observances, quotes scripture in council, enforces strict codes on retainers and family. Dutiful defender against “pagan” Battanians; leads longbow patrols and ambushes with grim resolve, viewing every raid as holy work. Widowed young (wife lost to forest sickness), channels grief into unyielding duty—runs Ormanfard meticulously: villages drilled in survival, chapel bells at dusk, skinwalker hunts relentless. Moral rigidity earns common respect, quiet resentment from freer spirits; barons admire steadfastness, find him inflexible.
Lady Adalindis dey Folcun — First child, eldest daughter, 20. Graceful and gentle, soft auburn hair, warm hazel eyes contrasting father’s sternness. Caring soul who loves animals—tends wounded birds/stray dogs, rides quietly, speaks softly to villagers and beasts. Skilled in light bowcraft and herbal lore; devout/charitable, aids poor and chapel rites with genuine kindness. Gentle nature softens Ormanfard’s harsh edges—eases retainer tensions, comforts skinwalker-scarred. Chafes quietly under Ecarand’s rigidity; seeks approval through duty while dreaming kinder path. Future likely dutiful marriage; compassion could strengthen ties—or pull toward mercy father deems weakness.
Lady Mauriana dey Folcun — Second child, younger daughter, 18. Slender/dark-haired, rebellious fire in green eyes, defiant chin tilt. Youthful rebel against father’s uptight moralism—questions rigid codes, slips out alone on forest trails, speaks sharply when piety feels stifling. Skilled mounted archer/tracker; devout privately—prays alone, aids villagers quietly—but chafes enforced chapel vigils and “never answer alone” rules. Defiance in small acts: sneaks food to suspected mutants, mocks zealous retainers, dreams freedom beyond border watch. Ecarand’s disapproval weighs heavy; seeks approval yet resists mold. Future uncertain—rebellion may lead scandal or forge fierce defender in own right.

dey Jelind
Cruel, debauched clan notorious for harsh rule and open excesses, ruling Hongard Castle — the legendary Black Tower — in the storm-lashed Biscan Alps of central-western Vlandia. The colossal cylindrical keep is hewn directly into the living rock of a sheer cliff face, rising like a blunt black finger above the high passes that guard the kingdom’s spine and the vital routes between the Black Timbervale and the upper Trand River. Under Jelind stewardship the fortress has become a grim den of vice: heavy tribute wrung from bound highland villages, feasts that spill into dawn with wine and worse, and rumours of mutant births under False Moon nights that add a sinister edge to their already dark reputation. Knights and men-at-arms of dubious standing wear coat-of-plates and favour maces or war-hammers for brutal close-quarters punishment; sergeants drill levies harshly, enforcing obedience through fear. It is said they are more akin to bandits with better trappings. Internal unity frays under indulgence — clan members pursue pleasure and power over cohesion — yet their calculated cruelty makes them feared on the border. Loyalty to King Derthert is nominal at best; they muster when forced, but whispers claim the Jelinds would sell the pass for the right price.
False Moon nights amplify the dread: shadows stretch unnaturally long in the torchlit halls, low knocking echoes rise from inside the mountain itself, and ghouls sometimes claw up from old battle sites in the valleys below, drawn to the scent of blood and excess.
Baron Vartin dey Jelind — Late twenties, clan leader. Lean and sharp-featured, with a cruel mouth and small, glittering eyes that miss nothing. Hard upbringing as the runt of the litter taught him to seize what he is owed; many older siblings met “convenient accidents,” paving his path to inherit Hongard. Savvy and underhanded, he relentlessly pursues selfish goals — brushing elbows with smugglers, poisoners, and black-market occultists. In hidden chambers beneath the Black Tower he pores over smuggled tomes on dark rites and Unveiling lore, seeking power through whispered pacts with shadows or veiled entities. Rumours swirl that he sold his soul to a witch for his ascent — whispers that are punished by impalement in the castle yard, bodies left as warnings. His cruelty is calculated; he enjoys power through fear, and the occult fuels his hidden ambition: Hongard is his kingdom, the crown merely an inconvenient overlord.
Lady Lietgardis dey Jelind — Early twenties, Vartin’s wife and consort. Tall and striking, raven hair coiled in elaborate braids, icy emerald eyes that freeze defiance. Pitiless and calculating, she enables the excesses while managing household intrigues with merchant precision, ensuring the coffers remain full. She shares her husband’s fascination with forbidden knowledge; her private study hides grimoires on curses, Unveiling mutations, and veil-thinning rituals. She experiments discreetly — blessing poisons with dark incantations or seeking prophetic dreams under the False Moon — to sharpen her edge in courtly schemes. Rumours persist that she is the witch to whom Vartin sold his soul; such whispers are crushed mercilessly, with accusers impaled at dawn. Her sharp mind keeps the clan afloat amid debauchery; she is the true steward of their wealth, secrets, and perhaps something darker. Respected and feared — none cross her lightly.
