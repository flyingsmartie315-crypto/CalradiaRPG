Ruling Clans Of Vlandia
dey Meroc
Weary yet benevolent royal house, ancient right holders of the throne. They command the rich Neurval region as their primary power base, with their seat at the ancient capital of Galend. They also hold direct authority over Sargot and Talivel Castle, along with a network of bound villages that supply the crown with recruits, taxes, and resources. As the royal clan, they embody Vlandian legitimacy amid baronial resentment and constant border pressures. Derthert’s measured rule favours diplomacy over endless war, earning deep love among the commons but quiet scorn from some nobles who view him as too soft.
Heraldry
A golden lion rampant upon a field of deep red — the ancient and undisputed colours of the Vlandian crown.
King Derthert dey Meroc — Mid-fifties, lean and scholarly with silver-gray swept-back hair, strong features, and a stern, intensely calculating gaze. He generally adorns himself in rich blue-and-red garments of fine velvet or quilted fabric, prominently featuring gold ornamental clasps, belts, and lion motifs, often paired with a high collar and royal cape or surcoat bearing the golden lion. He is frequently seen leaning over maps or strategy tables with coins and pieces. His exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood (full royal regalia for audiences and state functions, lighter blue-and-red doublet for private councils, ceremonial gold-accented armour for musters, etc.), but consistently radiates quiet royal authority and intellectual command. Quiet, commanding presence; his low voice never rises, but his steely winter-iron gaze stills the boldest baron. Once dreamed of conquest, now seeks peace through cold calculation and deep study of Imperial texts. The commons revere him for remembering widows and orphans; barons whisper he is too soft. Rules bellicose vassals with unflinching gaze, perfect memory of every slight and debt, and iron patience that lets feuds burn until their makers bend.
Queen Isolde dey Meroc — Early forties, consort to the King. Elegant and poised, with long wavy dark auburn hair and a calm, authoritative presence. She generally adorns herself in magnificent royal red velvet gowns with intricate gold embroidery, prominently featuring ermine fur trim (white with black spots) on the cloak and shoulders, a large ornate crown set with red and green gemstones, and often holding a golden scepter. Her exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood (full royal regalia for state functions, slightly simpler red-and-gold gowns for court, practical yet elegant attire for travel, etc.), but consistently radiates refined royal elegance and quiet authority. She is a skilled diplomat and administrator who manages the royal household and many courtly affairs with grace and precision. Her steady counsel has helped stabilize the realm during difficult times.
Crown Prince Morcon dey Meroc — Late twenties, heir apparent. Tall and broad-shouldered with dark wavy hair and a strong, commanding presence. He generally adorns himself in rich red-and-gold royal garments of fine velvet or quilted fabric, prominently featuring the golden lion rampant on the chest, often paired with a golden circlet or crown, a black velvet cape with gold trim, and a finely crafted sword at his belt. His exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood (full royal regalia for audiences and state functions, lighter red-and-gold doublet for private councils, ceremonial lion-emblazoned armour for tourneys or musters, etc.), but consistently radiates the martial prestige expected of the future king. Good warrior, commander, leader — held shield-walls, turned charges, drilled troops into war engines. Lance respected in Pravend’s lists; orders obeyed without question. Blunt to rudeness, uncharismatic in courtly way, speaks plainly and cuts through flattery. Wins fierce loyalty from sergeants and yeomen who value deeds; grates on proud vassals expecting deference. Stewardship learned managing royal villages with swift justice and fair levies.
Prince Erdurand dey Meroc — Late twenties, second son. Stout warrior, broad and powerful, prefers fighting on foot with massive claymore that sings through mail and plate. Brave to recklessness — led foot charges into Battanian ambushes, held ground when cavalry faltered. He generally adorns himself in martial plate-and-mail of deep red and gold, prominently featuring the golden lion rampant across the breastplate and a crimson cloak or tabard, often with practical leather gloves and sturdy boots. His exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood (full harness for raids and frontline duty, lighter red-and-gold tabard for training, ceremonial lion-emblazoned armour for tourneys, etc.), but consistently reflects the fierce, battle-hardened warrior of the royal line. Uninterested in governorship or stewardship; finds council tables stifling, prefers clarity of steel. Loyal to father and brothers, but blunt disinterest in politics makes him a wildcard.
Prince Alarys dey Meroc — Mid-twenties, third son. Very talented warrior with icy, aloof reputation that chills rooms. He generally adorns himself in elegant red-and-gold garments of fine velvet or quilted fabric, prominently featuring the golden lion and intricate scrollwork, often paired with a delicate golden circlet or crown and a black velvet or silk cloak. His exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood (formal court regalia for audiences, lighter gold-accented doublet for private councils, ceremonial red-and-gold armour for tourneys, etc.), but consistently radiates refined, almost aristocratic menace. Master of lance, sword, strategy — wins tourneys with precise, cruel efficiency. Well-learned in Imperial histories and Vlandian-rite theology, applies texts with ruthless pragmatism. Distant, self-contained; speaks little, observes much.
Princess Elys dey Meroc — Early-to-mid twenties, only daughter. Graceful, fair-haired, moves through court with quiet confidence in embroidered gowns. Well-learned — reads Imperial chronicles, knows heraldry, genealogy, subtle language of favors and alliances. Good-natured and warm where brothers are blunt or aloof; smiles easily, listens intently, remembers every name and grievance. Brokers understandings between fractious barons with gentle words and timely gifts.
Prince Amorcon dey Meroc — Late teens to early twenties, fourth son. Young and outwardly unremarkable — average height, plain but pleasant features, short brown hair, light stubble, and warm brown eyes that miss little. He generally adorns himself in practical yet finely made dark leather or velvet doublets with gold embroidery and scrollwork, often paired with a fur-trimmed collar, a heavy gold chain bearing a large medallion, and sturdy leather boots. His exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood (everyday dark doublet for estate duties, finer gold-accented velvet for court, practical riding gear for travel, etc.), but consistently reflects the quiet, reliable styling of a young royal who earns trust through deeds rather than flash. Honest nature slowly wins people over — speaks truthfully without guile, keeps word even when inconvenient, treats commons and knights with same respect. Stewardship through small duties — overseeing village granary, escorting minor caravans — where reliability quietly earns trust.
Romund dey Meroc — Distant cousin (third or fourth removed) of Derthert, late thirties to early forties. Broad-shouldered and imposing, bald with a short white beard and weathered face marked by decades of war. He generally adorns himself in heavy plate armour of deep blue and gold, prominently featuring the golden lion motifs across the breastplate and pauldrons, often with a crimson tabard or cloak and sturdy leather gloves. He is frequently seen carrying a finely wrought longsword. His exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood (full harness for training and guard duty, lighter blue-and-gold tabard for court, ceremonial lion-emblazoned armour for formal occasions, etc.), but consistently radiates the seasoned, battle-hardened authority of the royal master-at-arms. Renowned warrior — unhorsed champions in Pravend, led deep Battanian raids, held Sargot breach single-handed. Serves as Derthert’s master-at-arms and captain of the royal guard.
Chapter Forces
As the royal house, the dey Meroc maintain the largest and most prestigious standing force in Vlandia, numbering approximately 2,800–3,500 men (the Royal Army proper, excluding vassal levies). Their core strength is centred in Neurval around Galend (the capital), Sargot, and Talivel Castle, supported by a network of bound villages (including Furbec, Meroc, Nogrent, and several smaller hamlets in the Neurval valley). Because their holdings include the wealthy and strategically vital Neurval region (fertile valleys, trade routes, and key strongholds), they can afford higher pay, superior equipment, and constant training. Their forces are disciplined, loyal, and versatile — elite royal knights, professional men-at-arms, and well-equipped royal crossbow companies equally capable of field battles, siege defence, and rapid response to border threats. The Royal Army serves as both the kingdom’s ultimate deterrent and the standard by which all other Vlandian forces are measured.

dey Cortain 
Ambitious, entitled clan with extreme dynastic claims, tied to mercenary bands like the Golden Boar. Hold Charas with its affluent port and sunny bountiful land, pressing ancient rights to rival lands—most bitterly against dey Arromanc holdings—fueling grudges that threaten to ignite into open feud with Derthert himself. The clan’s knights ride under black-boar banners, ruthless in battle and relentless in council; their ambition is matched only by their arrogance, viewing other houses as lesser and the crown as a temporary obstacle. Wealth flows from Charas’ foundries and mercenary contracts, yet every coin is weighed against legacy and power.
Baron Ingalther dey Cortain — Early forties, clan leader. Arrogant, broad-shouldered, with a lion’s mane of dark hair streaked iron and a perpetual sneer that dares challenge. Possesses the means and experience to back his hauteur—veteran of Pendraic, commander of sellsword companies, skilled with lance and mace. Obsessed with clan legacy; every decision, every raid, every marriage serves to elevate Cortain above all others. Covets dey Arromanc lands with naked hunger, risking open feud with Derthert by pressing ancient claims in council and border skirmishes. Rules Charas like a fortress-treasury—forgers work night and day, coffers swell, but his recklessness threatens to overreach even his formidable strength.
Lady Elbet dey Cortain — Mid-forties, Ingalther’s wife and shrewd consort. Slender, dark-eyed, with sharp features and a calm that masks relentless calculation. Manages alliances and household with masterful precision—brokers marriages, negotiates mercenary contracts, tempers Ingalther’s recklessness with quiet counsel and timely reminders of consequences. Advances clan power through subtlety where her husband uses force; her network of informants and favors stretches from Galend to distant keeps. While Ingalther blusters, Elbet weaves the web that keeps Cortain ascendant—ruthless when needed, but always one step ahead.
Asela dey Cortain — First child, eldest daughter, early twenties. Raised with the high expectations of all Cortain children—groomed to rule or advance clan interests through marriage, counsel, or steel. Direct and unflinching; speaks plainly, cuts through flattery, commands respect with a level gaze and sharp tongue. Skilled in courtly intrigue and light lance-work; her poise hides a mind as ambitious as her father’s. Expected to secure powerful alliances or manage Charas’ trade networks; her future prestige tied to proving she can wield influence as ruthlessly as her parents.
Amalgun dey Cortain — Second child, eldest son, early twenties. Groomed to be Ingalther’s heir—very much his father’s son in arrogance and ambition. Broad and strong, with the same dark mane and commanding presence. Generous to friends and allies (lavish gifts, open purse), ruthless to enemies (swift retribution, no mercy). Accomplished knight—leads mercenary detachments with cold efficiency, wins tourneys when he deigns to enter. Stewardship lessons come through commanding Charas’ outlying garrisons; his future as heir is clear, yet his father’s obsession with legacy weighs heavily—Amalgun must prove himself worthy or risk being eclipsed by siblings or rivals.
Arthamund dey Cortain — Third child, second son, late teens. Often mistaken for Amalgun due to near-identical familial appearance—same broad build, dark curls, strong jaw—yet carries a more relaxed demeanor. Daring and generous; quick with a laugh, open-handed with coin or aid, charges into skirmishes with reckless joy. Less groomed for leadership than his brother, he thrives in the freedom of lesser expectations—leads small patrols, wins friends among yeomen and knights alike. His easy nature masks a sharp mind; future prestige uncertain but promising—generosity may win loyalty where Amalgun’s ruthlessness breeds fear.
Mitela dey Cortain — Fourth child, youngest daughter, late teens. The forgotten child—shy and bookish where siblings are bold. Plain-featured, dark-eyed, always with a tome or scroll in hand; prefers library corners to tourney yards. Quiet strong moral character—disgusted by her family’s underhanded dealings, yet too timid to openly defy them. Reads Imperial histories and Vlandian-rite texts obsessively, dreaming of a world governed by justice rather than greed. Stewardship lessons come through managing household libraries and minor accounts; her future uncertain—marriage alliance likely, but her conscience may yet prove a quiet fracture in the clan’s ruthless unity.

dey Arromanc 
Traditionalist house that shattered centuries of male-only succession by elevating a woman to leadership, holding Jaculan amid perpetual watchfulness against Battania’s shadowed forests and Mount Jacul’s mutant-haunted caves. Lands long coveted by Ingalther dey Cortain, fueling constant border tension and simmering vendettas. The clan prizes disciplined stewardship, martial competence, and fierce defense of inheritance; their knights and sergeants are known for steady shield-walls, heavy arbalest volleys, and unyielding loyalty to Derthert, born of shared contempt for reckless ambition. Bound villages supply grain and horses under a watchful, penny-counting hand—every coin tallied, every tithe enforced fairly but inexorably.
Baroness Calatild dey Arromanc — Mid-forties, clan leader and early widower (first husband fell in border clash); remarried to Uthery, who now stands at her side. Tall, stern-featured, dark hair streaked gray pulled into a severe braid, she favors practical riding leathers and mail over gowns. Competent, hard-working steward who counts every penny—ledgers balanced to the last brass, tithes collected with iron fairness, granaries kept full against siege or lean years. Rules Jaculan with steady resolve, overseeing drills, patrols, and village affairs herself. Defies Vlandian norms through sheer capability and unbreakable will; her sharp, unyielding gaze matches Derthert’s in council, though warmer toward loyal vassals. Remarriage to Uthery strengthened the clan’s martial core; she will not see her house diminished.
Lord Uthery dey Arromanc — Mid-forties, Calatild’s husband and consort. Strong, accomplished warrior and leader of men—broad-shouldered, battle-scarred, with close-cropped iron hair and a voice that carries across drill fields. Experienced commander who has led crossbowmen at Pendraic and turned desperate holds into victories through sheer grit. Brave and headstrong; charges first, trusts instinct over caution, and speaks his mind plainly even to barons who outrank him. Loyal to Calatild and the clan above all; his presence bolsters household troops and reminds covetous neighbors that Jaculan is defended by more than ledgers. His headstrong nature sometimes clashes with Calatild’s careful stewardship, yet their union is one of mutual respect and shared resolve.
Silvind dey Arromanc — First child, eldest daughter to Calatild and Uthery, mid-twenties. A fair maiden with striking features and long dark hair, graceful in silk yet quick with a dagger when needed. Knows exactly what she wants—be it a favorable marriage, a voice in council, or a say in her own future—and isn’t afraid to reach for it. Polite but unyielding; uses charm and sharp wit to navigate court, quietly advancing clan interests. Stewardship lessons come through managing household affairs and ladies’ courts; her ambition is tempered by loyalty to her mother. Future prestige tied to strategic alliances—many lords eye her as a prize, but Silvind intends to choose her own path.
Thomund dey Arromanc — Second child, eldest son to Calatild and Uthery, early twenties. Handsome, broad-shouldered, with easy dark curls and a ready smile that disarms even wary yeomen. Well-esteemed duelist—has unhorsed seasoned knights in Pravend’s lists with fluid grace and precise lance-work—and competent commander who leads small detachments with calm authority. Daring and friendly; quick to share a flask with sergeants, jest in the yard, charge first into skirmishes. Impulsive streak runs deep—rushes into fights or challenges without weighing odds, trusting steel and luck. Uninterested in governorship or stewardship; finds ledgers and council tables stifling, prefers tourney field or border patrol. Future prestige tied to martial renown—barons respect his blade, but whisper that a house led by Calatild needs a steadier hand than a dashing, impulsive heir.
Odofled dey Arromanc — Third child, second daughter to Calatild and Uthery, early twenties. Plain-looking with sharp features, dark eyes, and a perpetually furrowed brow. Sharp-minded and cantankerous; quick tongue slices through flattery and fools alike, often leaving courtiers red-faced. Sees through schemes others miss; her blunt observations have saved the clan from more than one poor alliance or hidden betrayal. Stewardship learned through managing household finances and village disputes—her ledgers rival Calatild’s, her judgments harsh but fair. Loyalty fierce, but her prickly nature makes her few friends outside family. Future prestige rests on her intellect—useful in council, yet her cantankerous streak risks alienating potential allies.
Lasand dey Arromanc — Fourth child, second son to Calatild and Uthery, late teens. Fair young man with open features, light brown hair, and warm hazel eyes that meet others without guile. Well-liked for honest, generous, and tender nature—shares bread with stable-boys, listens to villagers’ grievances, offers aid without expecting return. Brave despite lacking his brothers’ martial prowess; has stood firm in skirmishes and taken wounds without complaint. Stewardship lessons through small duties—overseeing village granaries, escorting minor caravans—where his fairness quietly earns trust. Barons dismiss him as soft; household and commons quietly admire him. Future prestige fragile but promising—honesty and kindness may yet prove rarer strengths than steel in a court of schemers.

dey Tihr
Notoriously avaricious and neglectful clan ruling Pravend and Drapand Castle, the ancient Imperial jewel now under Vlandian grip. Greed drives every decision; silver groschen flow from markets and tolls while bound villages languish in neglect and quiet resentment. The clan’s wealth is legendary, their loyalty to the crown questionable—many barons whisper that dey Tihr would sell the city’s marble if the price were high enough. Pravend’s forums and aqueducts stand as trophies of conquest, yet under Tihr stewardship they slowly decay, symbols of profit over pride.
Heraldry
A silver cross and diamonds glint over rich emerald green fields.
Duke Aldric dey Tihr — Mid-forties, clan leader. A bald, heavily built man with a thick mustache and goatee, his stern face and commanding presence reflect years of calculated ruthlessness. Broad-shouldered and muscular beneath ornate armour of deep emerald green and silver, he moves with the confidence of a man who has turned wealth into power. Jealously guards Pravend like a dragon its hoard; every coin, toll, and Imperial relic is tallied and protected with paranoid zeal. Ignored Derthert’s call at Pendraic to secure personal gain, earning lasting scorn from other barons. Rules with indolent cruelty—justice sold to the highest bidder, villages squeezed until they bleed silver.
Lady Ethild dey Tihr — Mid-forties, Aldric’s wife. A woman of refined elegance and commanding presence, with fair features, light eyes, and a composed smile that can shift from warm generosity to icy calculation in an instant. She generally adorns herself in magnificent emerald-green velvet gowns heavily embroidered with intricate silver thread featuring scrolling patterns and the Tihr cross, often complemented by a finely jeweled silver crown set with green gemstones and a long white embroidered veil that drapes gracefully over her head and shoulders. She is frequently seen carrying a small leather-bound ledger as a symbol of her meticulous stewardship. Her exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood, but consistently reflects the height of clan wealth and refined authority. Warm and generous to friends and allies, ice-cold and merciless to enemies, she is an exceptionally savvy steward and money-maker who oversees Pravend’s markets, tolls, and caravans with ruthless efficiency. Not shy about underhanded means—false weights, bribed officials, whispered threats, quiet poisonings when rivals or disloyal stewards become inconvenient. Her ledgers are flawless, coffers ever-growing; she is the true power behind the clan’s wealth while Aldric takes public credit.
Furnhard dey Tihr — First child, eldest son, mid-twenties. Slight build, almost delicate beside his father’s bulk, yet a good warrior and one of Pravend’s most accomplished jousters—precise, controlled, unnervingly accurate with lance and sword. Well-learned in Imperial histories and Vlandian-rite theology, quotes ancient edicts with chilling calm. A sadistic sociopath beneath the polished exterior; born under the False Moon (a secret Aldric has suppressed with gold and threats), his true nature emerges in private—cruel smiles during interrogations, lingering over wounded prisoners, quiet delight in pain. He adorns himself in numerous trappings of wealth, wearing fine plate armour accented with emerald green and gold (sunburst and diamond motifs), a rich emerald green cloak trimmed with black fur, and dark leather gloves. Masks his coldness masterfully with courtly manners and filial piety; only household guard whisper of the darkness behind his eyes. His future as heir is both asset and danger—skill wins tourneys, hidden nature risks scandal that could shatter the clan’s bought prestige.
Lady Liena dey Tihr — Second child, only daughter, mid-twenties. Widely regarded across Vlandia as the kingdom’s most eligible bachelorette, Liena is a vision of noble grace and beauty. She has vibrant auburn-red hair styled in elegant braids crowned with a delicate silver circlet, striking emerald-green eyes, and fair, flawless features. She generally adorns herself in luxurious emerald-green velvet gowns richly embroidered with intricate silver thread, prominently featuring the Tihr cross, fleur-de-lis, and ornate scrollwork, often accompanied by a finely crafted sword at her belt. Her exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood, but consistently reflects refined elegance and the height of clan wealth. Graceful and composed, she rides sidesaddle with poise, wields a light lance in exhibition jousts with surprising skill, and speaks of chivalry, honor, and divine right with sincere conviction. Courteous to all, charitable to the poor, and devout in Vlandian-rite observances, many lords see in her the ideal noble lady. Yet her honest and merciful nature—genuine compassion for the downtrodden and refusal to countenance cruelty even for profit—stands in stark contrast to the rest of her avaricious family, often placing her at quiet odds with them. Her hand in marriage is a highly coveted prize, a union that could either bind Pravend’s wealth to another house or fracture Tihr unity if her conscience proves stronger than blood.
Chapter Forces
The dey Tihr maintain a professional standing force of approximately 1,800–2,400 men. Shaped by the immense wealth of Pravend — the kingdom’s premier river port on the Trand River delta — their strength lies in well-equipped companies of heavy arbalest crossbowmen, disciplined men-at-arms, and a dedicated riverine flotilla of armed barges and galleys. Because their fiefs include the bustling port of Pravend (with its cranes, warehouses, grain mills, and constant trade traffic) and Drapand Castle guarding the vital river approaches, they can afford superior armour, constant training, and generous pay. Their forces are pragmatic and opportunistic — perfectly suited to protecting river trade convoys, suppressing unrest in the city, and projecting power along the Trand River. They excel at rapid response to riverine raids and controlling the lucrative tolls and shipping lanes that fuel the clan’s legendary wealth.

dey Gunric
Steady warrior upstart clan risen through merit, lords of Tirby Castle, holding strategic fiefs along contested borders. Loyal yet pragmatic, they balance knightly honor with hard-won survival in constant skirmishes—raids from Battania, probes from Empire remnants, winter attrition in the hills. Their knights and sergeants are disciplined and dependable, favoring heavy arbalest volleys and shield-wall stands; bound villages supply flax, clay and recruits under a fair but firm hand. Loyalty to Derthert runs deep—earned through shared campaigns rather than inherited oaths—yet they guard autonomy fiercely, refusing to waste men in glory-chasing feuds.
Heraldry
A black pike fish cresting from blue waves, set against a white sky.
Baron Ospir dey Gunric — Early thirties, clan leader. Steadfast and reliable soldier’s soldier—broad-shouldered, close-cropped brown hair flecked with gray from years afield, face weathered by sun and steel. He generally adorns himself in rugged plate-and-mail harness of deep blue and black, accented with white fur trim and the pike-fish crest on the breastplate and cloak, often paired with a heavy round shield or war axe. His exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood (full harness for border patrols, lighter leather-and-chain for court, ceremonial blue cloak for musters, etc.), but consistently reflects the hardy, practical warrior styling of the northern fiefs. Spent more time on campaign than in his own halls; commands with quiet competence, leading by example rather than bluster. Unmarried—delayed taking a wife due to border demands, though whispers say he waits for a match that strengthens the clan without entangling it in rivalries. Loyal to Derthert, musters promptly when called, quietly resents barons who risk men for personal prestige. Rules fiefs with soldier’s practicality—taxes fair, justice swift, training yards always active.
Sir Lucand dey Gunric — Late twenties, knight of common birth elevated through sheer renown in Ospir’s service. Lost his left eye to a Nord naval raider’s arrow during a brutal riverine boarding action—now covered by a plain black patch, the remaining eye sharp and watchful. He generally adorns himself in practical blue-and-black mail and plate with white fur accents and the pike-fish crest, often wearing a heavy sea-cloak or reinforced leather jerkin for shipboard fighting. His exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood (full harness for raids, lighter boarding gear for river patrols, simple blue tunic for camp), but consistently reflects the rugged, seafaring practicality of the northern fiefs. Steadfast, brave, and beloved by his men—cares deeply for those under his command, shares rations in lean times, ensures wounded are carried off the field. Naval commander by trade; leads river patrols and coastal raids from swift galleys, turning narrow waterways into kill-zones with archers and boarding parties. Common roots make him approachable—speaks plainly to yeomen and knights alike—yet his record silences doubters. Future tied to clan’s martial strength; loyalty and care for troops make him the heart of Gunric’s fighting men.
Lady Bertiliana dey Gunric — Early twenties, wife of Lucand. Daughter of a lesser lordling, married to strengthen ties between houses. Upstanding lady with quiet dignity—fair-haired, steady-eyed, always composed. She generally adorns herself in practical yet finely made wool and linen gowns of deep blue and white, trimmed with black fur and embroidered with the pike-fish crest, often paired with a simple silver brooch or belt. Her exact attire is subject to daily variation depending on occasion and mood (everyday wool for estate work, finer blue velvet for receiving guests, practical riding gear for travel), but consistently reflects the sturdy, practical styling of the northern fiefs. Manages household and bound villages with calm efficiency—ledgers balanced, granaries full, disputes settled fairly. While Lucand campaigns, she keeps home fiefs running smoothly, ensuring supplies reach the front and families of the fallen are cared for. Her steady hand complements her husband’s battlefield valor; respected by commons and retainers alike, she quietly bolsters the clan’s reputation for reliability in a realm where many houses fracture under greed or ambition.
Chapter Forces
The dey Gunric maintain a lean but highly disciplined standing force of approximately 950–1,150 men. Shaped by the rugged, resource-scarce Ebor Peninsula (flax, clay, silver mines, fishing fleets), their strength is built around hardy men-at-arms, heavy arbalest companies, and a small but effective riverine/coastal flotilla of galleys and longships. They excel at defensive shield-wall stands, rapid coastal raids, and protecting river trade routes. Because their fiefs are not especially rich, they rely on quality over quantity—well-trained, well-equipped troops who fight with the grim pragmatism of men who know every loss hurts.

dey Valant
Ruthless, craggy and hard like the mountains of the Ebor Peninsula, yet dangerously charming; a calculating clan in relentless pursuit of power. They hold Rovalt (a fortress-town hewn from the Rhennod Mountains) and Caleus Castle in the rugged peninsula, thriving on silver prospecting from Alantas, fur trade, and the griffon-haunted crags. Their knights train amid snow and stone, hunting griffon eggs and fledglings for prestige and mounts—only the righteous may hope to ride one, and Valant bloodlines claim that purity more often than most. Loyalty to Derthert is pragmatic, never sentimental; they serve when it profits, strike when it weakens rivals, and always keep one eye on the next opportunity in the peaks.
Heraldry
A black royal gryphon standing upon a red field, crested by a deep blue sky. The colours are black, red and indigo.
Duke Servic dey Valant — Late twenties, clan leader. Daring, devious, and coldly calculating, a lord and warrior of great renown across Vlandia. Lean and dark-haired, with a predator’s smile that disarms as easily as it threatens. One of the few proven griffon riders—his mount, a massive silver-gray beast named Stormclaw, is said to have carried him through blizzards and border raids alike. Disdains the mock combat of tourneys as child’s play; prefers true peril, where death’s edge brings a rare, genuine grin to his face and banishes the deep sadness that shadows his eyes at rest. That melancholy—whispered to stem from some old loss—lifts only in the face of great danger, when he rides laughing into storm or steel. Rules Rovalt with charm and iron; vassals follow willingly, enemies fall swiftly.
Sir Varmund — Early thirties, comrade-in-arms from Servic’s past and now knight and master-at-arms of the clan. Formidable warrior and leader of men—broad, scarred, with iron-streaked beard and a voice like grinding stone. Pragmatic and ruthless when need demands; renowned across the Ebor for recovering many griffon eggs from the highest, most perilous nests—dubbed “the Griffon Knight” for his daring climbs and the fledglings he has raised into loyal mounts. Commands Caleus Castle and the clan’s griffon-breeding efforts; trains young knights in the crags, teaching them to face heights and talons without flinching. Because Duke Servic rides a royal griffon and rules the land where the Knights Griffon maintain their primary residence, the Order has formally pledged one of their inner circle to serve as his aide and advisor. Sir Varmund holds this rare honour — the only person in the realm besides the King himself to receive such consideration from the Order. Steadfast to Servic, his presence steadies the clan’s more daring impulses with cold calculation.
Lady Alwith dey Valant — Late twenties, wife of Servic. Daughter of a wealthy Rovalt burgher—an eccentric artisan obsessed with metallurgy and craftsmanship—who taught her the value of measured caution and a devious streak learned from merchant parentage. Slender, dark-eyed, with a calm smile that conceals sharp thoughts; dresses in fine wool and subtle silverwork of her father’s forge. Manages household and trade ledgers with precision, turning silver ore and griffon feathers into profit while quietly watching for threats or opportunities. Her caution tempers Servic’s daring; her devious streak ensures no rival outmaneuvers the clan in back-alley deals or border negotiations. Respected, never underestimated.
Lady Ingeltrud — Early twenties, wife of Varmund. Daughter of a wealthy but minor noble house that owns shipping caravans along the western coast. Chapter Forces
The dey Valant maintain a professional standing force of approximately 1,100–1,450 men. Shaped by the rugged Ebor Peninsula (Rovalt as the main fortress-town and Caleus Castle as secondary stronghold, with bound villages Alantas for iron mining and Halisvust for grain), their strength lies in elite griffon-knights and mountain-hardened troops. They field companies of arbalest crossbowmen for devastating volleys in the crags, supported by heavy infantry trained for shield-wall stands in narrow passes. Because the region is not particularly wealthy (relying on silver prospecting, fur trade, and the prestige trade in griffon eggs and trained mounts), they emphasise quality, daring, and local knowledge over sheer numbers. Their forces are pragmatic and opportunistic — perfectly suited to raiding, border defence, and griffon-supported strikes in the high terrain.

dey Fortes

dey Molarn 
Quietly steadfast and honorable minor noble house ruling Ostican, a minor Imperial port turned seafaring haven on the western coast of the Ebor Peninsula in northern Vlandia. Ostican was once a quiet harbor where northern seafarers—many talented corsairs—found shelter, hired by Imperial factions to harry rivals during the latter Empire’s intermittent civil wars. Some of the first Vlandians to reach Calradia’s shores slipped into its back alleys, sending word to their kinsmen of riches ripe for conquest should an invasion come in earnest. Under dey Molarn, the town retains its seafaring soul: docks bustle with trade in fish, salt, timber, and olive oil; longshoremen and yeomen mingle with grizzled captains; the clan’s banner—two crossed arbalests on green and silver—flies over warehouses and training yards. This sigil harks to their founding: the original Molarn patriarch, an Imperial deserter turned mercenary, earned his lands by holding a vital river ford with disciplined arbalest volleys that broke a Battanian counter-attack. The weapon became the clan’s sigil—precision, reliability, and quiet lethality over flashy charges. The region around Ostican raises the most renowned arbalests in Vlandia: master craftsmen forge steel-prodded, cranequin-wound crossbows prized by sergeants and mercenary companies alike; many local yeomen and artisans ply the sellsword trade, sailing from Ostican’s docks to serve distant lords or return laden with coin and scars. Knights and sergeants favor practical coat-of-plates and these famed heavy crossbows; yeomen train with longbows and arbalests in coastal clearings. Bound villages supply grain, oil, and hardy folk under a measured hand that avoids excess. Loyalty to Derthert is sincere and unflashy—Molarn musters promptly, fights cleanly, returns home without seeking glory or grudges. In a kingdom of ambitious schemers, dey Molarn endures as a quiet pillar: dependable, unpretentious, and rarely noticed until needed.
Baron Hecard dey Molarn — Late forties, clan leader. Solid and unassuming in build, weathered brown hair cropped short, broad face etched by sun and sea wind, calm hazel eyes that miss little. Devoted to family and crown, avoids court intrigue and flashy tourneys, preferring the quiet work of maintaining stable lands. Respected by commons for fairness, quietly admired by other lords for reliability in muster and council.
Lady Adaltrud dey Molarn — Mid-forties, Hecard’s wife. Loved by bound folk and sailors for measured fairness, she is the hearth that keeps Molarn grounded amid the realm’s storms.

dey Rothad
Cautious, pragmatic house of middling lineage ruling Usanc Castle, a southern Imperial-border watchpost where intrigue and betrayals fester in shadowed courtyards. The clan holds the fortress through quiet loyalty and calculated self-preservation—neither ambitious schemers like Cortain nor rigid traditionalists like Arromanc. Knights and men at arms favor polearms and coat-of-plates for defensive formations; yeomen train in the castle yards with arbalests. Bound villages supply grain, wool and olives from the fertile Neruval valley, and recruits under a measured, sometimes stingy hand. Loyalty to Derthert is steady but conditional—Rothad musters reliably, yet guards autonomy fiercely, wary of overcommitment in border wars that drain coffers without gain. Unveiling shadows touch Usanc lightly: ghouls occasionally rise from nearby Imperial ruins on false-moon nights, forcing nightly burnings, while whispered betrayals feel amplified by the thin veil.
To the south of Usanc, the jagged mountains cradle wild griffons—eagle-headed, lion-bodied, massive-winged noble beasts nesting in high crags since the Unveiling. Their prey-rich isolation sustains them; roars echo through storms, shadows wheel unnaturally vast under false-moon light. Berican pursues their eggs with little success—offers lavish rewards in gold crowns and land grants, drawing desperate souls (mercenaries, hunters, reckless youths) to the perilous cliffs. Few survive long; most return maimed or never return, their bones bleaching among the rocks. Berican’s insecurity among prouder nobility—his house lacks ancient prestige or vast wealth—fuels this generosity: open purses and promises compensate for whispered doubts about Rothad’s standing, yet the griffon chase remains a costly obsession that yields only rumors of near-misses and lost men.

dey Folcun 
Merciful yet rigidly dutiful minor noble house ruling Ormanfard Castle on the Battania border. Loyalty to Derthert is absolute—rooted in duty and piety—yet Folcun’s uptight righteousness sets them apart: no tolerance for excess, no mercy for pagan “savages,” and constant vigilance against Unveiling corruption. False Moon nights thicken dread—knocking from walls, unnatural silence, stretching shadows—prompting chapel vigils and blessed charms over every door.

dey Jelind
Proud and insular highland clan, masters of the storm-lashed Biscan Alps. They hold the legendary Hongard Castle — the Black Tower — a colossal cylindrical keep hewn directly into the living rock of a sheer cliff face. This isolated mountain stronghold guards the kingdom’s central-western spine and the vital passes between the Black Timbervale and the upper Trand River. The dey Jelind are known for their grim resilience and cold pragmatism; they view the thin veil that plagues their peaks as both curse and forge. Wealth flows from controlling mountain trade routes, iron mines, and the hardy alpine herds, but their true strength lies in the unbreachable walls of Hongard and the loyalty of their mountain-hardened retainers.

Chapter Forces
The dey Jelind maintain a hardened mountain force of approximately 1,100–1,400 men. Shaped by the treacherous Biscan Alps and the thin veil that haunts Hongard, their troops are veterans of False Moon sieges, wraith incursions, and brutal highland skirmishes. They favour heavy polearms, crossbows, and close-quarters maces/hammers for fighting in narrow passes and on castle walls.
